CARE: can a whole-slide model learn the tissue regions it should reason over?

Paper2Html2026-06-25CVPR 2026 digest entry | Medical AI | Pathology foundation modelSource: arXiv:2602.21637v3 PDF
Medical AIComputational PathologyAdaptive Region ModelingWhole-Slide ImagesMolecular AlignmentROI FeaturesMathJax checked

The short version. Whole-slide pathology models usually inherit a patch-first habit: they see a slide as thousands of square tiles, then ask an aggregator to recover tissue structure afterward. CARE asks whether the unit between patch and slide should be learned instead: irregular adaptive regions that tend to follow tissue organization. Its pipeline freezes a CONCH v1.5 patch encoder, learns an Adaptive Region Generator (ARG), runs self-attention inside each region, and pools regions into either a whole-slide embedding or a selected region-of-interest (ROI) feature. The model is pretrained in two stages: iBOT-style self-supervision on 34,277 whole-slide images, then CLIP-style alignment to 13,289 WSI-RNA pairs and 8,225 WSI-protein pairs. The payoff is concentrated in molecular and survival tasks, where CARE leads most category averages; on morphology it ties or trails TITAN (see §9 for where the claim stops). The claim is architectural, not magical: better regions help molecularly guided slide representations, but ROI features are not universally better than whole-slide features. Read the paper as a region-tokenization argument for computational pathology: if patches are character-level tokens, CARE tries to learn the word boundaries of tissue.

One-line contributionCARE turns fixed patch or grid chunks into learned adaptive regions, then uses molecular profiles to make those regions biologically meaningful.
Key mechanismThe Adaptive Region Generator (ARG) routes each patch into one of its 3 nearest candidate regions by spatial inclusion and feature similarity; a Region Structuring Loss (RSL) stops the routing from collapsing onto the nearest region; and Semantic-and-Prior Fusion (SPF) pools regions by mixing area coverage with gated attention.
Core evidenceAcross the category averages in Table 1 (the 33 tasks grouped by type), CARE is strongest on most molecular and survival settings, while morphology is mixed and must be read by head and metric.
Reproduction boundaryThe PDF gives the backbone, pretraining stages, A100 count, downstream GPU, and hyperparameters. It does not state wall-clock pretraining time or total GPU-hours.

1. Motivation: why is fixed chunking a pathology problem?

A whole-slide image (WSI) is too large to process end to end at native resolution, so computational pathology systems cut it into patches. A patch encoder then turns each small tile into a feature vector, and a slide-level model pools those vectors into one prediction. This recipe is practical, but it hides a biological mismatch.

Pathologists do not diagnose a slide by treating every square patch as an independent token. They first look for tissue structures and regions of interest. A tumor nest, a necrotic focus, or an area with nuclear atypia may have an irregular boundary. A square grid can split that structure apart or merge it with background tissue.

Q: Why not let a Transformer recover the right regions from all patches?

Guide: separate two jobs. One job is representing local morphology. The other is deciding which local pieces belong to the same tissue unit.

A: A global aggregator receives thousands of patch features after the patch boundaries have already been fixed. It can reweight patches, but it does not explicitly create a new region unit that follows tissue structure. CARE inserts that missing unit. It first proposes square subregions as anchors, then reassigns patches into irregular adaptive regions before slide pooling.

Takeaway: CARE is not just another slide aggregator. Its central move is to make the patch-to-region step learnable.

2. Contributions: what the paper actually adds

The authors state three contributions. First, CARE is a pathology foundation model whose slide representation is built from adaptive regions rather than raw patch bags or fixed grids. Second, the Adaptive Region Generator partitions a WSI into irregular but morphology-relevant regions, and the same regions can be used for ROI-level analysis or pooled into a whole-slide feature. Third, the pretraining pipeline starts with self-supervised morphology learning and then aligns slide features to RNA and protein profiles, so the learned regions are nudged toward biological signals.

The larger reader-side interpretation is narrower than "CARE is best everywhere." The paper's strongest claim is that adaptive regions plus molecular alignment improve data efficiency for slide-level pathology representations. The results support that claim most clearly on molecular prediction and parts of survival analysis. Morphological classification is competitive, but TITAN and FEATHER still win some matched heads and metrics.

How to read "about one-tenth of the data"

CARE uses 34,277 WSIs for self-supervised pretraining. That is about 10.2% of TITAN's 335,645 WSIs and 5.8% of PRISM's 587,196 WSIs in the paper's architecture table, but it is not one-tenth of every baseline. FEATHER and TANGLE are listed with about 24,000 TCGA pan-cancer slides because their papers did not report specific counts.

3. Data: morphology first, molecular profiles second

The pretraining data come from TCGA and GTEx. TCGA supplies formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded H&E cancer slides, while GTEx supplies normal-tissue slides. The paired molecular data are smaller, so CARE uses them after the self-supervised stage rather than as the only supervision source.

4. Pipeline: patch features become adaptive regions before slide pooling

The input is a WSI scanned at 20x magnification. CARE first performs tissue segmentation, tiles the tissue into square patches, and encodes each patch with a frozen CONCH v1.5 patch foundation model. From that point on, the trainable part of CARE works on patch features, not raw pixels.

The first trainable stage defines regular square subregions on the patch grid. These subregions are only anchors. ARG then lets each patch choose among its top-3 candidate subregions using both spatial closeness and feature similarity. The result is an adaptive region: a set of patches that may no longer form a perfect square.

The second stage runs self-attention inside each adaptive region. This is local to the region, so a patch interacts with related neighboring patches rather than all slide patches. The final stage uses SPF to combine the adaptive region features into a WSI embedding. The same SPF weights also identify the highest-weighted adaptive region as the ROI feature.

Q: What does molecular guidance change in this pipeline?

Guide: the molecular profile is not used to annotate each patch. It is a slide-level biological signal paired with the WSI.

A: The RNA and protein profiles teach the slide embedding which visual regions correlate with molecular state. Because the slide embedding is pooled from adaptive regions, that pressure flows back to the region generator and region features. It does not create pixel-accurate molecular labels, but it does bias the representation toward tissue areas that carry biological signal.

Takeaway: Molecular guidance acts at the slide level, but CARE's region bottleneck gives it a place to localize.

5. Method: a soft routing problem with an anti-collapse loss

CARE starts from patch features \(f_j \in \mathbb{R}^d\), one per patch. It defines \(M\) square subregions \(S_i=(x_i,y_i,k,g_i^{CLS},g_i^Q)\), where \((x_i,y_i)\) is the top-left patch coordinate and \(k\) is the window side length in patches. The paper's selected value is \(k=8\). Each subregion has two descriptors: \(g_i^{CLS}\), produced by regional self-attention with a learnable CLS token, and \(g_i^Q\), produced by a learnable query that cross-attends to patches inside that subregion.

5.1 Soft inclusion: make nearby anchors plausible, not final

For patch \(j\), let \(t_j=(u_j,v_j)\) be its patch anchor. For subregion \(i\), let \(s_i=(x_i,y_i)\) be the top-left anchor. CARE converts anchor distance into a soft inclusion score:

$$ d_{ij}=\lVert t_j-s_i\rVert_2,\qquad \mu_{ji}=1-\frac{d_{ij}}{\max_{0\le j'\le N} d_{ij'}},\qquad C=[\mu_{ji}]^{N\times M}. $$
Eq. 1: spatial soft inclusion

How to read it: \(\mu_{ji}\) is high when patch \(j\) is spatially close to subregion \(i\). This is not the final assignment. It only limits the candidate set so that a far-away subregion does not steal a patch on semantic similarity alone.

5.2 ARG: choose among top-3 nearby candidates

For each patch \(j\), CARE keeps only the top-3 subregions under \(C_{ji}\). Call that candidate set \(I_j\). For each candidate \(i\), it computes four cosine similarities:

$$ c_{ji}=\left[ \cos(f_j,g_i^{CLS}), \cos(f_j,g_i^Q), \cos(f_j^R,g_i^{CLS}), \cos(f_j^R,g_i^Q) \right]. $$
ARG score ingredients

Here \(f_j^R\) is the subregion-aware patch feature after regional self-attention. The four scores compare raw and context-enriched patch features against both subregion descriptors.

$$ \tilde{s}_{ji}^{(m)}=\frac{\exp(s_{ji}^{(m)})}{\sum_{i'\in I_j}\exp(s_{ji'}^{(m)})}, \qquad \rho_{ji}=\frac{1}{4}\sum_{m=1}^{4}\tilde{s}_{ji}^{(m)}. $$
Eq. 2: normalize each similarity type over the candidate set, then average

The regional selection score combines semantic affinity and spatial inclusion:

$$ w_{ji}=\rho_{ji}C_{ji},\qquad A^\star(j)=\arg\max_{i\in I_j} w_{ji},\qquad A_i=\{j\mid A^\star(j)=i\}. $$
Patch-to-region assignment

How to read it: \(A_i\) is the adaptive region assigned to anchor \(i\). The hard assignment happens only after two filters: top-3 spatial candidates and semantic scoring within those candidates.

5.3 ARSA and RSL: represent each region, then stop routing collapse

Once patches have been assigned to adaptive region \(A_i\), CARE builds a region sequence \(F_i^{AR}=\{f_j\mid j\in A_i\}\). It prepends a learnable \([CLS]^{AR}\) token and runs adaptive region self-attention:

$$ \left[g_i^{AR};F_i^{AR,cep}\right]= \operatorname{ARSA}\left([CLS]^{AR}\Vert F_i^{AR}\right). $$
Eq. 3: adaptive region feature

The output \(g_i^{AR}\) is the adaptive region representation. The sequence \(F_i^{AR,cep}\) contains context-enriched patch features for patches inside that region.

A routing model can collapse into a trivial habit: always choose the nearest candidate or concentrate too many patches into one easy region. CARE adds Region Structuring Loss (RSL) to control the average candidate rank selected by the router:

$$ E=\mathbb{E}_{j\sim \operatorname{Unif}\{1,\ldots,N\}} \mathbb{E}_{i\sim \pi_j}[r_j(i)] =\frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N}\sum_{i\in I_j} w_{ji}r_j(i), $$
Eq. 4: mean expected selected rank
$$ L_{RSL}=(E-E^\star)^2,\qquad L_{total}=L_{main}+\lambda_{RSL}L_{RSL}. $$
Eq. 5: region structuring objective

Here \(r_j(i)\in\{0,1,\ldots,K-1\}\) is the rank of candidate \(i\) for patch \(j\), with 0 being the highest-scoring candidate, \(K=3\), and \(E^\star=0.5\) in the selected hyperparameters. This target does not force every patch to choose the second candidate. It keeps the global routing distribution away from always picking rank 0 or always chasing far candidates.

5.4 SPF: pool regions using area and semantic attention

SPF has two weights for each adaptive region. The coverage prior \(\alpha_i\) is the fraction of patches in the region. The semantic weight \(\beta_i\) is computed by gated attention:

$$ s_i=w^\top\left(\tanh(Vg_i^{AR})\odot\sigma(Ug_i^{AR})\right), \qquad \beta_i=\frac{e^{s_i}}{\sum_k e^{s_k}}. $$
Eq. 6: gated semantic attention

The final WSI embedding is the weighted sum of region features:

$$ z_{WSI}=\sum_{i=1}^{M} \left(\lambda_{SPF}\alpha_i+(1-\lambda_{SPF})\beta_i\right)g_i^{AR}. $$
Eq. 7: Semantic and Prior Fusion

How to read it: \(\alpha_i\) prevents the model from ignoring the size of a region, while \(\beta_i\) lets a small but important region receive high weight. CARE sets \(\lambda_{SPF}=0.5\), so the selected model gives equal top-level weight to coverage and semantic attention.

Q: Do the ablations prove that adaptive regions, not just molecular pretraining, cause the gains?

A: They make that explanation plausible. Table 3 changes the region generator while holding the broad setting fixed, and the full ARG is best on most columns. Table 4 shows that the anti-collapse and fusion parameters have sensible middle optima. Table 5 then adds the molecular curriculum on top of the region model. The pieces support each other, but no single table proves a universal win.

Takeaway: The mechanism is best read as a three-part stack: adaptive routing, anti-collapse pressure, and molecular alignment.

Q: What is the most likely overstatement?

A: "CARE solves WSI representation learning." The paper supports a narrower claim: adaptive regions plus molecular alignment can produce strong, data-efficient slide representations, especially for molecular tasks. It does not show that every diagnosis, every cohort, or every clinical endpoint should be handled by ROI features.

Takeaway: CARE is a strong region-modeling argument, not a blanket clinical deployment claim.

10. Summary: the useful mental model

In one sentence: CARE treats tissue regions as learnable tokens, then uses molecular profiles to bias those tokens toward biologically meaningful slide evidence.

Three takeaways

  1. The missing unit is the region. Patches are too local and whole-slide pooling is too late, so CARE inserts adaptive regions between them.
  2. The routing is constrained, not free-form. Top-3 spatial candidates keep assignments local, cosine scores add semantics, and RSL keeps the selected rank distribution away from trivial collapse.
  3. The evidence is strongest when biology matters. CARE's molecular and survival averages are compelling, but morphology and ROI claims need matched-head, metric-by-metric reading.

A practical reading path is: start with Figure 1 for the tokenization analogy, then work through Eq. 1-7 to see how regions are formed and pooled, then read Tables 1-5 before the heatmaps so the visualization does not carry more weight than the benchmarks allow.
