CURE: can better sampling and anatomy-level supervision make radiology VLMs less eager to hallucinate?

Paper2Html2026-06-25CVPR 2026 digest entry | Medical AI | PUC Chile / CENIA / iHEALTH / KAUSTSource: arXiv:2601.15408v2 PDF

The short version. CURE asks whether a medical vision-language model can become more reliable by reorganizing existing public chest-X-ray supervision, rather than by adding private data or a larger backbone. The failure it targets is concrete: a grounded radiology model may locate the requested anatomy yet still attach an abnormal finding that is not present. CURE's first move is anatomy-grounded report generation, or AGRG: ask the model about one anatomical region at a time and train it on both normal and abnormal descriptions. Its second move is an error-aware curriculum: after validation, tasks or classes with higher current error receive more sampling weight in the next training stage. The payoff is concentrated in grounding: on the Chest ImaGenome AGRG benchmark, CURE more than doubles localization IoU over MAIRA-2 (0.601 vs 0.249), and it wins all six phrase-grounding IoU columns in Table 2. The reliability story is real but uneven. Mean abnormal-finding hallucination drops sharply (26.5% to 8.8%), yet one anatomy regresses outright (Cardiac Silhouette) and several full-report text metrics stay mixed (notably RadGraph F1, where CURE trails CXRMate). The per-metric and per-anatomy splits are in §7. So the defensible claim is reliable anatomy-grounded reporting, not a blanket fix for medical-VLM hallucination.

One-line contributionCURE is a curriculum-guided multi-task recipe that gives MedGemma-4B-IT grounding behavior by changing task form and sampling weights; the CURE run uses public chest-X-ray sources, while the paper notes that some MAIRA-2 report-generation comparisons benefit from proprietary USMix pretraining.
Key mechanismAGRG changes the prompt distribution from "ground an abnormal phrase" to "locate and describe this anatomy," so normal regions become explicit supervision. Error-aware sampling then spends more of the limited training budget on tasks and classes where the model is currently weak.
Core evidenceIn AGRG, CURE improves IoU to 0.601 ± 0.008 from MAIRA-2's 0.249 ± 0.008 on the Chest ImaGenome subset; the same method also reduces mean abnormal hallucination from 26.50% to 8.78%, but the Cardiac Silhouette row moves the other way, 25.67% versus 2.00%.
Reproduction boundaryThe supplement states MedGemma-4B-IT with LoRA rank 16, 4-bit NF4, BF16, effective batch size 25, learning rate 2e-4, one NVIDIA RTX A6000, and about 45 hours for the final 9,000-step run.

1. Motivation: why does grounding training create false positives?

A medical vision-language model (VLM) reads an image and produces language. In radiology report generation (RRG), that language should be tied to the image evidence. Visual grounding is the part of the task that connects a phrase, such as "left clavicle," to the image region that supports it.

Grounding helps only if the model also knows when a region is normal. The paper argues that standard phrase-grounding data is biased because a phrase is usually annotated when it names a finding. The model repeatedly sees a query phrase paired with a box around an abnormality. That can turn the query itself into an abnormality cue.

CURE attacks two imbalances at once. The first imbalance is between data sources: Chest ImaGenome can yield more than 12.9 million potential AGRG instances, while MS-CXR has only 815 training phrase-grounding instances. The second imbalance is inside each source: some anatomies and finding classes appear much more often than others. A static sampler can therefore overtrain what is common and undertrain what is clinically rare.

Q: If the old signal is biased, why not just collect more normal-region annotations?

Guide: the paper is trying to isolate a training-method effect. If extra expert labels were added, the gain could come from the new labels rather than the curriculum or task reformulation.

A: CURE keeps the data sources fixed and changes how they are used. AGRG asks about anatomical regions directly, so the same Chest ImaGenome scene graph yields examples where the correct answer is normal. The curriculum then changes how often each source or class is sampled, based on validation error. The model gets a new shape of supervision without new images or new expert annotation.

Takeaway: The paper's lever is data organization. It does not claim that data no longer matters; it claims that the existing data had been fed in a biased and unbalanced form.

2. Contributions: what CURE adds, and what it does not

The authors state three contributions. First, CURE is an error-aware curriculum framework that updates sampling probabilities from current model performance. Second, it transfers grounding behavior to MedGemma-4B-IT, a base medical VLM that the paper treats as lacking built-in visual grounding. Third, it reduces mean abnormal hallucination across six key Chest ImaGenome anatomies by 18.6 percentage points, from 26.50% with MAIRA-2 to 8.78% with CURE, while losing the Cardiac Silhouette row, 25.67% versus 2.00%, and slightly losing Right Lung, 11.67% versus 10.67%.

The useful narrowing is that CURE is not a new model architecture. The backbone is MedGemma-4B-IT, adapted with LoRA, a low-rank fine-tuning method that trains small adapter matrices rather than all model weights. The novelty is the training recipe: AGRG supplies normal and abnormal anatomy-level descriptions, and the curriculum rebalances tasks and classes from validation error.

How to read the headline numbers

"Better" in this page always names the metric and setup. CURE clearly improves grounding IoU in Tables 2-4. It does not dominate every text metric: on PadChest-GR GRG, CURE has higher IoU than MAIRA-2, 0.265 versus 0.256, but lower F1-Mi, 0.507 versus 0.591, and lower CXRFEScore, 0.574 versus 0.616.

3. Data and metrics: three training sources, four evaluation tasks

CURE trains on three public chest-X-ray sources. Chest ImaGenome is derived from MIMIC-CXR and links anatomical regions to bounding boxes and report text. PadChest-GR contains reports with grounded phrases. MS-CXR provides phrase bounding boxes from MIMIC-CXR. VinDr-CXR is held out for zero-shot evaluation, meaning the model is tested there without training on it.

The task abbreviations are load-bearing. PG, phrase grounding, asks the model to locate a phrase in the image. GRG, grounded report generation, asks for a report whose findings are linked to bounding boxes. AGRG, anatomy-grounded report generation, asks for one anatomical location at a time, either to locate it, describe it, or do both. RG, report generation, asks for a standard full report.

Intersection over Union (IoU) measures box overlap between a prediction and a reference box; higher is better. CXRFEScore is the paper's clinical semantic-similarity metric for radiology text; higher is better. CheXbert F1, Precision, Recall, and Cosine Similarity score label-level clinical findings extracted from reports. RadGraph F1 scores extracted radiology entities and relations, while RaTEScore scores medical-entity semantic agreement.

4. Pipeline: train, evaluate, reweight, repeat

The pipeline starts by converting heterogeneous supervision into one instruction format. Each sample becomes an image, an instruction, and a response. Bounding boxes use normalized center coordinates and size, written as [center x, center y, width, height]. This format lets PG, GRG, and AGRG live in one multi-task fine-tuning loop.

Q: Why use validation error to pick the next samples instead of using the training loss?

Guide: a high training loss can mean "hard and useful," but it can also mean noisy, mislabeled, or already overfit. The paper uses task metrics on validation subsets because the target behavior is grounding and clinical text fidelity.

A: CURE wants the sampler to follow the metric failures that matter at evaluation time. If a source has low IoU, its localization behavior is weak. If a source has low CXRFEScore, its clinical text alignment is weak. Converting those failures into sampling probability makes the curriculum a feedback controller over the reported metrics.

Takeaway: The curriculum is not "easy to hard" in the usual sense. It is "currently weak to more often sampled."

5. Method: error-normalized sampling plus anatomy-level negatives

5.1 The baseline sampler is open loop

Standard multi-task training samples each data source according to its size. That is simple, but it ignores model performance. In CURE's setting it would overrepresent Chest ImaGenome because that source can produce millions of AGRG instances.

$$p_i=\frac{|D_i|}{\sum_j |D_j|}$$
Baseline sampler: source \(D_i\) is sampled in proportion to its number of examples.

How to read it

\(p_i\) is the probability of sampling source \(D_i\). The numerator is data volume, not difficulty. If the largest source is already easy, this sampler still keeps feeding it.

5.2 CURE turns validation error into sampling probability

For each data source, CURE computes a score from localization and text fidelity. The paper uses IoU for localization and CXRFEScore for clinical text similarity.

$$s_i=\alpha\cdot \mathrm{IoU}_i+(1-\alpha)\cdot \mathrm{CXRFEScore}_i$$
Eq. 1 from the paper: a task-adaptive score for source \(D_i\).

The error is the missing score, \(e_i=1-s_i\). A source with high score has low error and receives less weight. A source with low score has high error and receives more weight.

$$p_i=\frac{e_i}{\sum_{j=1}^{K} e_j}$$
Eq. 2 from the paper: normalize errors into the next-stage sampling probabilities.

How to read the two equations

\(\alpha\) controls which metric matters. The supplement states \(\alpha=1\) for pure localization, \(\alpha=0\) for text-only generation, and \(\alpha=0.8\) when both localization and text are evaluated. The final probability is a normalized error, so all probabilities sum to one.

5.3 AGRG supplies the missing normal-region signal

The curriculum changes how often examples appear. AGRG changes what an example means. Instead of asking the model to ground a finding phrase, AGRG asks it to locate, describe, or locate-and-describe a named anatomical location. Chest ImaGenome makes this possible because its scene graphs link anatomies to boxes and report snippets.

This gives the model negative evidence in the ordinary clinical sense: many named regions are normal, and the correct description should say so. That directly addresses the Figure 1 failure. If the model has seen "left clavicle" paired with normal descriptions, the query no longer implies a fracture.

Q: Why do the curriculum and AGRG need each other?

A: AGRG fixes the content bias by adding normal anatomy descriptions to the training task. The curriculum fixes the allocation problem by making rare or weak sources appear often enough to matter. AGRG without balancing can still be drowned by source size or class frequency. Balancing without AGRG can still repeat the same abnormal-only phrase-grounding signal.

Takeaway: CURE's mechanism is two-part: change the label geometry, then change the sampling pressure.

6. Training and compute: what the paper discloses

The supplement gives a concrete training recipe. The implementation uses PyTorch, Hugging Face, and TRL's SFTTrainer on a SLURM-managed HPC cluster. The final CURE run uses one NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU with 48 GB VRAM and 60 GB system RAM.

7. Experiments: grounding wins first, text metrics are mixed

The experiments separate four questions: can CURE locate phrases, can it generate anatomy-level descriptions, can it write grounded reports, and does this reliability survive in full report generation? The strongest answer is on localization. The more cautious answer is on report text: CURE often improves grounding and hallucination behavior while leaving several text metrics to MAIRA-2 or CXRMate-RRG24.

Phrase grounding is evaluated by macro and micro IoU. Macro IoU averages classes; micro IoU aggregates instances. CURE improves over MAIRA-2 on all three datasets in Table 2, including VinDr-CXR where both rows are zero-shot for this paper's CURE training sources.

Q: Is the curriculum itself the whole story?

A: No. The table says the task reformulation is already powerful, because v1 beats MAIRA-2 on AGRG IoU without augmentation, curriculum, or CIG pre-training. The curriculum and pre-training then improve the weaker tasks and the final grounding columns. The result is a stack of effects, not one magic sampler.

Takeaway: The strongest causal story is additive: anatomy-level supervision creates the right signal, and curriculum plus tuning decide how reliably that signal reaches all tasks.

Q: What is the easiest way to oversell CURE?

A: To say it solves medical VLM hallucination without extra data. The supported claim is narrower: with the same public CXR sources, AGRG and error-aware sampling substantially improve anatomy-level grounding and reduce mean abnormal-finding hallucination in a six-anatomy test. The tables also show anatomy-specific failures and text-metric losses.

Takeaway: CURE is best understood as a reliability-oriented training recipe for grounded CXR reporting, not a general clinical deployment guarantee.

10. Summary: the useful mental model

In one sentence: CURE makes the model see anatomical queries as normal-or-abnormal clinical questions, then uses validation error to decide which questions deserve more training budget.

Three takeaways

  1. AGRG fixes the label shape. A region query no longer means "find an abnormal phrase"; it means "say what is true about this anatomy."
  2. Error-aware sampling fixes the budget. The probability \(p_i=e_i/\sum_j e_j\) turns weak validation performance into more future samples.
  3. The evidence is strongest for grounding and reliability. IoU improves sharply, and mean hallucination improves with anatomy-level exceptions, while full-report text metrics remain mixed.

A good reading path through the paper is: start with Figure 1 to see the false-positive failure, read Eq. 1-2 to understand the sampler, then read Tables 2, 3, 6, and 7 before using Table 5 to place the report-generation numbers in context.
