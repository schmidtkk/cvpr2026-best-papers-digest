IBISAgent: turning biomedical segmentation into a click, observe, and refine loop

CVPR 2026Medical AIAgentic MLLMBiomedical segmentation
MedicalSegmentationMLLMReinforcement LearningMask Refinement

IBISAgent asks whether a multimodal large language model, or MLLM, can segment biomedical objects by acting more like an annotator than a one-shot mask decoder. The paper's answer is to keep the language model in text space: it writes reasoning, emits text-form click commands, calls an interactive segmentation tool, observes the new mask, and repeats. Intersection over Union (IoU), the overlap between a predicted mask and the ground-truth mask divided by their union, is the main segmentation metric. Dice similarity coefficient (DSC) measures mask overlap with a different normalization, and F1 measures whether the segmented entity matches the target entity. Across the paper's three benchmarks, IBISAgent's IoU runs from 72.09 to 85.58, while the strongest released-weight medical MLLM baseline reaches only 35.62 to 50.74, and a different baseline leads each set. The low end, 72.09, is the held-out in-house set, the hardest of the three because its cases sit outside the training distribution; there IBISAgent also reports DSC 83.78 and F1 91.76 against that baseline's IoU 35.62. That is a large gap, but it is not a claim of clinical deployment readiness: the model uses Qwen2.5-VL-7B, a frozen segmentation tool, a two-stage training pipeline, and private held-out data. The interesting claim is narrower and testable: iterative visual reasoning plus dense click-level rewards can make an MLLM better at pixel-level biomedical grounding without adding a special segmentation token.

Core moveSegmentation is a multi-step decision process, not a single <SEG> token decoded into pixels.

Q: What is the root question?

Guide: compare a one-shot segmentation model with a human annotator. The model predicts once; the annotator sees the current error and places the next corrective point.

A: The paper asks whether an MLLM can learn that annotator-like loop for biomedical segmentation. IBISAgent makes the MLLM choose text-based clicks and lets a segmentation tool turn those clicks into masks.

Takeaway: The paper is strongest when read as a study of action policy and feedback, not as a new pixel decoder.

1. Motivation: why not just emit a segmentation token?

Recent medical MLLMs often adapt the LISA pattern: insert a special segmentation token, project its hidden state, and decode it into a mask with an external vision decoder. That works, but it couples the language model and the pixel decoder during fine-tuning. The paper argues that this coupling has two costs. First, it can disturb the model's ordinary text output space and risk catastrophic forgetting. Second, it still tends to make segmentation a single forward pass, while medical annotation is often an iterative process.

The biomedical setting makes the one-shot assumption fragile. A lesion can be low contrast, a structure can be thin or fragmented, and a mask can be almost right but still clinically unhelpful at the boundary. In those cases, the useful action is not "predict a final mask now." It is "look at the current mask, decide what kind of error remains, place a positive point in a missed region or a negative point in an over-segmented region, and ask the tool to update the mask."

Q: Why does the paper treat interaction as part of the method rather than an interface detail?

Guide: the next click depends on the previous mask. If the previous mask leaks outside the object, the right next action is different from the action after an under-segmented mask.

A: The click is a decision conditioned on feedback. Once the mask comes back as an observation, segmentation becomes a policy problem: choose the next step that improves the mask.

Takeaway: The motivation is not only better masks; it is giving the MLLM a feedback channel where pixel-level mistakes become visible before the final answer.

2. Contributions: what the paper actually adds

The paper states three contributions. First, it presents IBISAgent, an agentic framework that gives MLLMs fine-grained pixel-level visual reasoning without architectural modifications or implicit segmentation tokens. Second, it builds a large training collection from existing biomedical masks by simulating clicks, generating question-answer pairs, synthesizing reasoning traces, and adding self-reflective correction trajectories. Third, it trains the policy with cold-start supervised fine-tuning followed by reinforcement learning with segmentation-specific rewards.

The reader can infer a fourth, more general idea: segmentation tools can be treated as callable environments. The MLLM does not have to be the mask generator. It can be the policy that decides where to prompt the mask generator. That separation explains why the paper spends so much space on action schemas, observations, and rewards.

Comparison scope

When Table 1 reports parenthesized values for LISA, LISA++, SAM4MLLM, and VisionReasoner, those are baselines further fine-tuned on the authors' SFT and RL data. The unparenthesized values are released-weight runs. That setup distinction is kept wherever those rows are compared.

Q: What should count as the novelty?

Guide: do not look only for a new backbone. Look for the new interface between the language model, the segmentation tool, and the reward signal.

A: The novelty is the formulation. The MLLM writes actions, receives mask observations, and is trained to improve the next action rather than to emit a hidden token for a decoder.

Takeaway: IBISAgent is a policy-learning paper for biomedical masks, with MedSAM2 acting as the environment tool.

3. Datasets: how final masks become action trajectories

The paper starts from BiomedParseData, which contains 3.4 million image-mask-label tuples across 82 biomedical object types and 9 imaging modalities. Those tuples have final masks, not human click histories. IBISAgent therefore simulates annotation trajectories: at each step, the click simulator compares the current predicted mask with the ground truth, finds the largest false-negative or false-positive error region, and places the next positive or negative click near the center of that region.

The cold-start dataset contains 47,146 individual images or slices and 456,795 visual question-answer (VQA) pairs after filtering. The appendix reports average IoU 0.9427, median IoU 0.9507, average DSC 0.9703, median DSC 0.9747, and average trajectory length 8.69. The reinforcement-learning corpus contains 60,826 pixel-level samples and 564,385 VQA pairs across 8 modalities and 39 segmentation tasks. The listed common medical VQA datasets add 324,000 more VQA pairs, so the listed RL components sum to 888,385 pairs, reported in the main text as 888K.

Q: Does this dataset make the agent a human annotator?

Guide: the trajectories are simulated from final masks, then paired with generated questions and generated reasoning.

A: No. It gives the model a dense imitation signal for an annotator-like process, but the click histories are algorithmic and the reasoning traces are synthesized.

Takeaway: The data pipeline is powerful because it turns static masks into action supervision; it is also a source of reproduction cost.

4. Pipeline: the think, action, observation loop

Given an image \(I\) and a user question \(Q\), IBISAgent generates a pixel-space reasoning path \(P=\{(r_t,a_t,o_t)\}_{t=1}^{T}\). Each step contains textual thinking \(r_t\), an action \(a_t\), and an observation \(o_t\). A click action can contain one or more triples: target class, click attribute in \(\{+1,-1\}\), and normalized coordinate in \([0,1]^2\). The segmentation tool receives all previous clicks, the original image, and the previous mask logits, then returns a new mask.

$$r_{t+1}, a_{t+1} \sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot \mid I,Q,P_{\lt t})$$
Eq. 1: at each step, the policy chooses the next reasoning text and action from the image, question, and previous trajectory.

The observation is created by overlaying the updated mask on the image. That composite image is appended to the trajectory in an observation field. The next policy call can therefore see both the anatomy and the current segmentation state in one frame. The loop ends when the model emits a termination action and then a final answer.

Q: Why feed the updated mask back as an image observation?

Guide: the MLLM cannot improve a mask unless it can see what the last action changed.

A: The overlay makes the current mask part of the next visual input. The model can then decide whether the mask is missing tissue, spilling into background, or already good enough.

Takeaway: The observation turns segmentation quality into visible state, which makes multi-step correction possible.

5. Method: dense rewards for a sparse mask problem

The paper optimizes the policy with group relative policy optimization (GRPO) and removes the Kullback-Leibler (KL) penalty term used in some RL variants. Each prompt gets \(G\) rollout paths. The reward of a path is normalized against the rewards of other paths in the same group, so the policy learns from relative quality.

$$\mathcal{L}_{RL}=\mathbb{E}\left[-\frac{1}{G}\sum_{i=1}^{G}\frac{1}{N_i}\sum_{t=1}^{T_i}\min\left(\rho_{i,t}A_i,\operatorname{clip}(\rho_{i,t},1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon)A_i\right)\right]$$
Eq. 2: GRPO-style clipped objective over rollout paths; \(N_i\) excludes observation tokens.
$$\rho_{i,t}=\frac{\pi_{\theta}(r_{i,t},a_{i,t}\mid I,Q,P_{i,\lt t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}(r_{i,t},a_{i,t}\mid I,Q,P_{i,\lt t})},\qquad A_i=\frac{S_i-\operatorname{mean}(\{S_j\}_{j=1}^{G})}{\operatorname{std}(\{S_j\}_{j=1}^{G})}$$
Eq. 3: the policy ratio and group-normalized advantage used by the paper.

The reward is the average of five components:

$$S=\frac{1}{5}\left(S_{\mathrm{ans}}+S_{\mathrm{format}}+S_{\mathrm{click}}+S_{\mathrm{pseg}}+S_{\mathrm{len}}\right)$$
Eq. 4: final reward from answer, format, click placement, progressive segmentation, and trajectory length terms.

The answer and format rewards make the output parseable and task-correct. For closed-ended VQA, \(S_{\mathrm{ans}}\) checks exact match. For segmentation, the paper uses MedSAM2 to turn predicted clicks into a mask and gives a piecewise reward from the resulting IoU.

$$S_{\mathrm{ans}}(A,\hat A)=\mathbb{I}(A=\hat A)$$
Eq. 5: exact-match answer reward for closed-ended questions.
$$S_{\mathrm{ans}}= \begin{cases} 3, & \operatorname{IoU}\gt0.80,\\ 2, & 0.70\lt\operatorname{IoU}\le0.80,\\ 1, & 0.50\lt\operatorname{IoU}\le0.70,\\ 0, & \mathrm{otherwise.} \end{cases}$$
Eq. 6: segmentation answer reward; stronger overlap gets stronger positive feedback.

The click-placement reward is the first truly spatial part. Let \(a_t=(c_t,p_t)\), with coordinate \(c_t\in\mathbb{R}^{2}\) and click type \(p_t\in\{+1,-1\}\). The eligible false-negative and false-positive regions are:

$$\Omega_{\mathrm{FN}}=M_{\mathrm{gt}}\setminus M_{t-1},\qquad \Omega_{\mathrm{FP}}=M_{t-1}\setminus M_{\mathrm{gt}}$$
Eq. 7: missed target area and over-segmented area relative to the ground-truth mask.
$$S_{\mathrm{click}}(a_t)= \begin{cases} r_{\mathrm{click}}, & p_t=+1\wedge c_t\in\Omega_{\mathrm{FN}},\\ r_{\mathrm{click}}, & p_t=-1\wedge c_t\in\Omega_{\mathrm{FP}},\\ -\lambda_{\mathrm{miss}}, & \mathrm{otherwise,} \end{cases} \qquad r_{\mathrm{click}}=1,\ \lambda_{\mathrm{miss}}=1$$
Eq. 8: positive clicks should land in missed regions, and negative clicks should land in excess regions.

The progressive reward checks whether the step improved the mask. This is the temporal part: not just "was the click plausible," but "did the mask become better after the click?"

$$\Delta Q_t=\operatorname{IoU}(M_t,M_{\mathrm{gt}})-\operatorname{IoU}(M_{t-1},M_{\mathrm{gt}}),\qquad S_{\mathrm{pseg}}=\mathbb{I}(\Delta Q_t\gt0)$$
Eq. 9: a step earns progressive reward only if IoU strictly increases.
$$S_{\mathrm{len}}= \begin{cases} r_{\mathrm{eff}}, & T\le T_{\mathrm{opt}},\\ -\gamma(T-T_{\mathrm{opt}}), & T\gt T_{\mathrm{opt}}, \end{cases} \qquad r_{\mathrm{eff}}=1,\ \gamma=0.2$$
Eq. 10: efficient completion is rewarded; extra steps past the threshold receive a linear penalty.

Q: Why do the click and progressive rewards need to be separate?

Guide: a click can be in a sensible error region but still fail to improve the tool's mask, and a mask can improve even if the action policy has not learned the right spatial rule.

A: \(S_{\mathrm{click}}\) teaches where a corrective click belongs. \(S_{\mathrm{pseg}}\) teaches whether the action actually helped after the segmentation tool ran. One reward checks spatial validity; the other checks temporal improvement.

Takeaway: The method makes a sparse final-IoU problem learnable by rewarding the action and its consequence at every step.

6. Training: what is disclosed, and what is not

IBISAgent is built on Qwen2.5-VL-7B, and MedSAM2 is the default interactive segmentation tool. The paper trains on 16 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. Cold-start supervised fine-tuning uses learning rate \(1\times10^{-5}\), batch size 256, and 10 epochs. RL uses VERL, batch size 256, 4 candidate reasoning paths per question, at most 20 actions, learning rate \(1\times10^{-6}\), maximum context length 32K tokens, and 12 epochs.

Q: Do the experiments prove the gain comes from the tool alone?

Guide: Table 3 attaches MedSAM2 to several MLLMs, and Table 9 changes the tool inside IBISAgent.

A: No. Tool access by itself is weak in Table 3. The stronger evidence is that the same tool becomes much more effective when the policy is trained to place iterative corrective clicks.

Takeaway: The result is best explained by the learned interaction policy, with the segmentation tool as a necessary but insufficient component.

Q: What do the ablations say the mechanism is?

Guide: separate three questions: can the base model act at all, can RL improve it, and do the dense rewards explain the efficient multi-step behavior?

A: Cold-start SFT makes the action format usable. RL adds the largest single jump over the prompt baseline. The dense rewards then explain why the final policy improves masks with fewer steps.

Takeaway: The strongest causal evidence is Table 5: spatial and temporal rewards change both mask quality and trajectory length.

Q: What would be the easiest overstatement?

Guide: look for the difference between benchmark superiority and deployment readiness.

A: The overstatement would be "IBISAgent solves medical segmentation." The supported claim is narrower: under the reported 2D biomedical benchmarks, an MLLM trained as an iterative click policy has higher reported metrics than the listed one-shot or tool-calling baselines.

Takeaway: The method is promising as an annotation-style assistant, but the paper leaves latency, private evaluation, 3D extension, and full cost accounting open.

10. Summary: the useful mental model

In one sentence: IBISAgent moves biomedical segmentation from hidden-token mask decoding to an explicit loop: reason in text, click in image coordinates, observe the tool-generated mask, and refine until the model decides to stop.

Three takeaways

  1. The interface matters. The model keeps ordinary text outputs and uses text-form actions, while the segmentation tool handles pixels.
  2. The rewards make the loop learnable. \(S_{\mathrm{click}}\) teaches where to click, \(S_{\mathrm{pseg}}\) teaches whether the click improved the mask, and \(S_{\mathrm{len}}\) teaches when to stop.
  3. The evidence is strong but scoped. Table 1 and Table 3 show large segmentation gains, while Table 2 and the limitations show the cost of multi-turn interaction.

Q: What should a reader carry into the next medical-agent paper?

Guide: ask whether the agent receives useful state after each action, and whether the reward tells it what was wrong with the last action.

A: The key test is whether the agent has a feedback loop. Without observation and dense rewards, tool use can collapse into one-shot prompting. With them, the model can learn an annotation policy.

Takeaway: The paper's durable idea is not MedSAM2 usage; it is making pixel-level correction a sequence of visible, rewarded decisions.
