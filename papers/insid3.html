INSID3: training-free in-context segmentation from DINOv3 alone

Paper2Html 2026-06-26 CVPR 2026 Top Paper (candidate) Source: arXiv:2603.28480 PDF
In-Context Segmentation DINOv3 Training-Free Dense Self-Supervision Positional Debiasing MathJax checked

1. Lead

The short version. In-context segmentation, or ICS, asks a model to see one annotated support image and then segment the same visual concept in a query image. The support image is the example with a mask; the query image is the image to be segmented. This is harder than ordinary one-shot classification because the model needs two abilities at once: cross-image semantic matching, which finds the same concept across images, and intra-image region grouping, which turns matched patches into one coherent mask. Prior systems either fine-tune a vision foundation model, or VFM, which can narrow generalization, or combine DINOv2 with the Segment Anything Model, or SAM, which adds a large mask-supervised component and fixes much of the mask granularity through SAM. INSID3 argues that a frozen DINOv3 backbone already contains both ingredients, if its cross-image features are first corrected for positional bias. Positional bias here means that patches at the same image coordinate can score as similar even when their semantics differ. The paper removes that bias with a training-free orthogonal projection, then clusters raw DINOv3 features, selects a seed cluster with debiased cross-image matching, and expands it by combining semantic similarity with self-similarity. On the main 1-shot average over nine segmentation columns, INSID3 reports 55.1 mIoU, where mIoU means mean Intersection-over-Union; that is +7.5 points over the best training-free baseline, GF-SAM, which scores 47.6 mIoU. (A stricter like-for-like gap, against a GF-SAM rebuilt with the same DINOv3 backbone and debiasing, is smaller but still positive; the experiments give it.)

SetupOne annotated support image and one query image. The concept can be an object, a part, or a personalized instance.
Core claimA single frozen DINOv3 model can support both correspondence and mask formation without a decoder, fine-tuning, SAM, mask supervision, or category supervision.
Key fixEstimate a positional subspace from one noise image, project it out, and use the debiased features only for cross-image matching.
Important caveatINSID3 is not numerically best on every single dataset column. COCO-20i is stronger for fine-tuned SegIC and published GF-SAM, and X-Ray is stronger for SegGPT.

2. Motivation: why ICS needs both matching and grouping

ICS is a reference-conditioned segmentation problem. The reference mask says what the concept means in this episode, not what a fixed label vocabulary means in training. One episode may ask for a whole dog. Another may ask for a dog ear. A third may ask for one particular object instance among distractors.

That setup creates a two-part requirement. First, the model must match the support concept to the query image. Second, it must know where the matched concept stops inside the query. A good point match is not enough, because the output is a mask. A good generic mask proposal is not enough either, because the desired granularity comes from the support annotation.

The paper describes two dominant approaches. Fine-tuned methods such as SegIC and DiffewS inject segmentation behavior into a VFM through task-specific supervision. They can do well in-domain, but the paper reports drops when the test distribution moves away from the training distribution. Training-free systems such as Matcher and GF-SAM keep generalization by combining DINOv2 correspondence with SAM masks. That avoids downstream fine-tuning, but it brings a large two-model architecture and inherits mask-level supervision from SAM pretraining.

Q: why is DINOv3 different enough to revisit the design?

A: The paper argues that DINOv3 is not only a stronger semantic matcher. It also has dense, localized patch features that group into coherent regions within one image. That means the same feature space may handle both cross-image matching and intra-image grouping.

Takeaway: INSID3 is a minimality test: if DINOv3 already carries both signals, SAM and a trained decoder may be unnecessary for this task.

3. Contributions: what the paper adds

The paper's first contribution is the INSID3 pipeline itself. It performs ICS with one frozen DINOv3-L backbone, 304 million parameters, no segmentation decoder, no SAM, no fine-tuning, and no mask or category-level supervision for INSID3.

The second contribution is a diagnosis of DINOv3's positional bias. Cross-image similarity maps can light up target patches that share the support coordinate rather than the support semantics. The authors estimate a low-dimensional positional subspace from a noise image and remove it with an orthogonal projection. Orthogonal projection here means subtracting the component of each feature that lies in a chosen subspace, leaving only the component in the perpendicular complement.

The third contribution is the segmentation recipe built around the two feature views. Raw DINOv3 features keep their spatial regularity for clustering and self-similarity. Debiased features are used for reverse correspondence and cross-image semantic similarity. Reverse correspondence means each query patch asks which support patch is its nearest neighbor; the patch is kept as a candidate only if that nearest support patch lies inside the support mask.

The fourth contribution is evidence across one-shot semantic, part, personalized, 5-shot, semantic correspondence, backbone, SAM3, empty-mask, and runtime evaluations. The headline +7.5 mIoU average is against published GF-SAM on the nine main 1-shot columns. The fairer comparison to the authors' own GF-SAM† plus debiasing row is +6.3 mIoU, because that row already imports DINOv3 and the paper's debiasing into a SAM-based baseline.

4. Datasets and metrics

The main one-shot table covers nine columns. Six are one-shot semantic segmentation datasets: LVIS-92i, COCO-20i, ISIC2018, SUIM, iSAID-5i, and Chest X-Ray. Two are one-shot part segmentation datasets: PASCAL-Part and PACO-Part. One is one-shot personalized segmentation: PerMIS.

mIoU is the main segmentation metric. It measures the intersection between predicted and ground-truth masks divided by their union, then averages across cases. The semantic-correspondence study uses SPair-71k and PCK@T. PCK means percentage of correct keypoints; a prediction is correct when it lands within a normalized distance threshold T from the ground-truth keypoint.

Input images are resized to 1024 by 1024 for the main ICS experiments, following SAM-based baselines. The final masks are predicted at patch resolution, bilinearly interpolated to the original resolution, and refined with a conditional random field, or CRF. A CRF is a post-processing model that encourages nearby pixels with compatible appearance to share labels.

5. Pipeline: one feature space, two uses

INSID3 starts by extracting dense patch features from the frozen encoder $\Phi$. The paper writes the support and query features as

$$ F_r=\Phi(I_r), \qquad F_t=\Phi(I_t). $$
Eq. 1: $I_r$ is the support image, $I_t$ is the query image, and $F_r,F_t \in \mathbb{R}^{P \times D}$ are D-dimensional patch embeddings over P patches.

The pipeline then splits into two feature views. Raw features $F_t$ are used to cluster the query into regions, because their local spatial structure is useful. Debiased features $\tilde F_r$ and $\tilde F_t$ are used for correspondence, because absolute coordinate information can be harmful when two different images are being matched.

A seed is the first query cluster selected as the most likely part of the support concept. The seed is often only a partial localization, such as the head of a person or the neck of a giraffe. The final step asks which other candidate clusters should be merged with that seed.

6. Method: remove coordinate bias, then aggregate clusters

6.1 Positional subspace removal

The paper's diagnosis starts from a simple similarity map. Average the support features inside the support mask to form a prototype $p_r$, then score each query patch by its inner product with that prototype. DINOv3 captures semantics, but the same map can also show bright activations at matching absolute coordinates. That is positional bias.

INSID3 estimates that subspace once from a Gaussian noise image. The steps are:

$$ I_{\mathrm{noise}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1), \qquad F_{\mathrm{noise}}=\Phi(I_{\mathrm{noise}}), \qquad F_{\mathrm{noise}}=U\Sigma V^\top, \qquad B=[v_1,\ldots,v_s]. $$
Eq. 2: $B$ contains the top s right singular vectors of the noise-image features and is treated as a basis for positional directions.

The debiased support and query features are then projected onto the orthogonal complement of that basis:

$$ \tilde F_r = F_r\left(I_D-BB^\top\right), \qquad \tilde F_t = F_t\left(I_D-BB^\top\right). $$
Eq. 3: $I_D$ is the D-dimensional identity matrix. $BB^\top$ projects a feature onto the positional subspace, so $I_D-BB^\top$ removes that component.

The paper fixes the debiasing rank to $s=500$. It uses raw features for clustering and self-similarity, but debiased features for all cross-image matching steps.

6.2 Clustering and reverse correspondence

For the query image, INSID3 partitions raw DINOv3 features into disjoint clusters $\{G_1,\ldots,G_K\}$. The paper uses agglomerative clustering with threshold $\tau=0.6$. K-means would require choosing K up front; DBSCAN is described as brittle in high-dimensional feature spaces. Agglomerative clustering gives one threshold that controls granularity.

$$ \bigcup_{k=1}^{K} G_k=\Omega, \qquad G_i \cap G_j=\emptyset \quad \forall i \ne j. $$
Eq. 4: the clusters cover all patch indices $\Omega$ and do not overlap.

Candidate localization uses reverse correspondence. For each query patch $i$, INSID3 finds the nearest support patch $j$ in debiased feature space. It keeps query patches whose nearest support patch lies inside the support mask:

$$ \mathrm{NN}(i)=\arg\max_{j\in\Omega}\left\langle \tilde F_i^t,\tilde F_j^r \right\rangle, \qquad C_{\mathrm{NN}}=\left\{i\in\Omega \mid M^r_{\mathrm{NN}(i)}=1\right\}. $$
Eq. 5: reverse matching uses the query patch as the question and the support image as the search space. $M^r$ is the support mask.

A query cluster becomes a candidate if it overlaps $C_{\mathrm{NN}}$. This step uses negative evidence from the unmasked support background: if a query patch's nearest support neighbor lands outside the mask, it is not reliable support for the concept.

6.3 Seed selection and multiplicative aggregation

INSID3 next computes debiased prototypes for each candidate cluster and the support mask, then chooses the seed cluster with the highest cross-image score:

$$ \tilde p_k^t = \frac{1}{|G_k|}\sum_{i\in G_k}\tilde F_i^t, \qquad \tilde p_r = \frac{1}{|R|}\sum_{j\in R}\tilde F_j^r, \qquad s_k^{\mathrm{cross}}=\left\langle \tilde p_k^t,\tilde p_r \right\rangle, \qquad G^*=\arg\max_{G_k\in C_{\mathrm{cand}}}s_k^{\mathrm{cross}}. $$
Eq. 6: $R$ is the support foreground patch set; $C_{\mathrm{cand}}$ is the set of candidate clusters; $G^*$ is the seed.

A seed alone is usually too small. To recover the full concept, INSID3 combines cross-image semantic similarity with intra-image self-similarity. The self-similarity score uses raw query features, because raw DINOv3 features preserve the local region structure that the method wants for grouping.

$$ \bar p_k^t=\frac{1}{|G_k|}\sum_{i\in G_k}F_i^t,\qquad s_k^{\mathrm{intra}}=\left\langle \bar p_k^t,\bar p_*^t \right\rangle, \qquad S_k=s_k^{\mathrm{cross}}\cdot s_k^{\mathrm{intra}}, \qquad M_{\mathrm{final}}=G^*\cup\left\{G_k\in C_{\mathrm{cand}}\mid S_k\ge \alpha\right\}. $$
Eq. 7: $\bar p_*^t$ is the raw-feature prototype of the seed cluster, $S_k$ is the multiplicative aggregation score, and $\alpha=0.2$ is the merge threshold.

Why multiplication matters

The product requires a cluster to satisfy both tests. A region that matches the support semantically but is structurally far from the seed is down-weighted. A region that sits near the seed in the query but does not match the support concept is also down-weighted. Addition would let one high score compensate for one weak score; the paper chooses multiplication to make both signals load-bearing.

7. Training: none for INSID3 itself

INSID3 itself is training-free. The debiasing projection is computed from feature statistics of a noise image, not learned from masks or labels. Clustering, reverse correspondence, seed selection, aggregation, interpolation, and CRF refinement are also inference-time procedures.

DINOv3 is not trained by INSID3. The paper describes DINOv3 as a self-supervised VFM trained on massive-scale image corpora, with significant data and model scaling, a Gram anchoring objective, and high-resolution post-training. The exact pretraining data composition for DINOv3 is not stated in the INSID3 paper.

The three scalar hyperparameters are selected once on the COCO-20i training split with 3-fold cross-validation and then fixed across semantic, part, and personalized segmentation. There is no dataset-specific or task-specific tuning reported for the main INSID3 runs.

9. Ablations: the projection and aggregation both matter

Debiasing across DINO generations

The appendix directly compares DINOv2 and DINOv3 under the same semantic-correspondence protocol. Debiasing changes DINOv2 by only +0.3 PCK at each reported threshold, but changes DINOv3-Base by +3.1, +5.0, and +6.6. This supports the paper's claim that the positional-bias issue is much more visible in DINOv3.

The empty-mask result matters because many SAM-based methods always return a non-empty mask. INSID3 can return empty by subjecting the seed itself to the aggregation criterion: if no cluster has enough cross-image evidence, the output stays empty.

10. Limitations and caveats

The supplement states four limitations. First, INSID3 handles one target concept at a time. Multiple concepts require separate reference prompts. Second, the support prompt is a mask; the paper does not provide a point-prompt or box-prompt version. Third, the current formulation produces semantic masks, not separate instance masks for each matched object. Fourth, final quality depends on the representation learned by the frozen self-supervised backbone.

There are also reading caveats from the tables. INSID3 is strongest in average generalization and in non-COCO, non-X-Ray columns, but it does not win every cell. The authors' debiased GF-SAM† row is not prior work; it is a diagnostic baseline that gives SAM-based pipelines the same DINOv3 and debiasing ingredients. Comparisons to SegIC, DiffewS, SINE, SegGPT, and Painter are not supervision-matched, because those methods use task-specific training or semantic and mask supervision.

Finally, the paper's no-supervision claim applies to INSID3 and to the use of frozen DINOv3 features in this pipeline. SAM-based baselines include mask-supervised pretraining. DINOv3 itself is a pretrained self-supervised VFM; INSID3 does not train it.

11. Summary

In one sentence: INSID3 shows that frozen DINOv3 features can be turned into an in-context segmenter by removing positional bias for cross-image matching, clustering raw features for region proposals, and multiplying semantic alignment with self-similarity to grow a seed into a mask.

Three takeaways

  1. The method is training-free, but not feature-free: it relies on the dense self-supervised structure of DINOv3.
  2. The positional-bias correction is small but load-bearing. It improves semantic correspondence by up to +6.6 PCK and raises COCO-20i segmentation from 54.5 to 57.6 mIoU in the no-debias versus one-noise-SVD comparison.
  3. The aggregation step is what turns a matched part into a full mask. On COCO-20i and PASCAL-Part, self + cross aggregation beats thresholding, one-cluster selection, and cross-only aggregation.
