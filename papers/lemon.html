LEMON: how public surgical video becomes a foundation-model dataset

Paper2Html2026-06-25CVPR 2026 | Medical AI | King's College LondonSource: arXiv:2503.19740v4 PDF
Medical AIEndoscopic DatasetSurgical Foundation ModelAugmented DINO DistillationConvNeXtMathJax checked

The short version. LEMON asks whether public endoscopic videos can replace private pretraining corpora for a surgical foundation model. Its answer has two parts. First, it filters roughly 18,000 YouTube videos down to 4,194 curated surgical videos — 938 hours, 85 million frames, 35 procedure types. Second, it trains LemonFM, a ConvNeXt-L model, with an augmented form of DINO, a self-supervised teacher–student method. The augmentation is the one real idea: the student must match the teacher not only across crops of one frame, but also across adjacent frames and across same-procedure frames from other patients. The headline result is clean — with the backbone frozen (linear probing) and with it fine-tuned, LemonFM tops every column of Tables 2, 3, and 4. The one honest soft spot is the half-data setting: the 50%-shot model still beats every surgical foundation model, but against task-specialist phase models it is mixed, trailing Trans-SVNet and LoViT on Cholec80 (78.7 vs 79.3 Jaccard, 92.1 vs 92.4 accuracy). The page states exactly where it loses rather than rounding it away.

1. The core problem: public video is abundant, but not yet training data

Surgical computer vision has a familiar bottleneck. Public datasets are often small, narrow, or task-specific. In the paper's opening comparison, many surgical datasets contain fewer than 100 videos and less than 30 hours of footage, and many surgical image datasets contain fewer than 10,000 images. That scale can validate one benchmark, but it is too narrow to train a foundation model, meaning a pretrained model meant to transfer across many tasks and hospitals.

Private operating-room data is hard to collect because it carries patient privacy, consent, and annotation burdens. Public web video looks like an escape route. The catch is that a YouTube search result is not an intraoperative training clip. It can contain lectures, testimonials, slides, surgeon faces, out-of-body views, logos, and on-screen user-interface elements. If those artifacts stay in the dataset, a discriminative model can learn shortcuts that have nothing to do with anatomy or instruments.

Q: Why is curation a scientific claim here rather than just cleanup?

Guide: separate "large public video collection" from "large surgical visual corpus." The first is easy to collect. The second has to remove non-surgical content at several granularities.

A: The paper's wager is that public video becomes useful only after a strict funnel. Whole videos can be non-surgical. Surgical videos can still contain non-surgical segments. Surgical frames can still contain non-surgical regions. LEMON addresses all three levels, then tests whether the curated version improves downstream transfer. In Table 6, curation adds 4.5 F1 points on AutoLaparo phase recognition and 1.3 mDice points on CholecSeg8k segmentation compared with an uncurated LEMON version.

Takeaway: The dataset contribution is not just size. The load-bearing claim is that a public web corpus needs a video-to-frame-to-region filter before it can act like surgical pretraining data.

2. What the authors actually claim

The paper states three main contributions. First, it proposes a multi-stage curation pipeline for online surgical videos. The pipeline filters non-surgical videos, trims non-surgical segments, removes non-surgical frames, and obliterates non-surgical regions inside otherwise surgical frames. Second, it releases LEMON, a dataset with 4,194 videos, 938 hours, 85 million frames, and 35 minimally invasive procedure types, including robotic and non-robotic laparoscopy. Third, it trains LemonFM on LEMON with augmented knowledge distillation and evaluates it on four downstream task families across six external datasets.

The paper also adds two video-level tasks on LEMON itself. One is multi-label procedure classification over 35 procedure types. The other is binary surgery-type classification, where the output is robotic or non-robotic. The authors use those labels both to validate dataset breadth and to build LemonFM-Vid, a video classifier that aggregates LemonFM frame embeddings.

How this page reads "state of the art"

When a table is mixed, the page states the mixture. Table 2 is clean: LemonFM is best in all reported linear-probing columns. Tables 3 and 4 are clean for the full LemonFM row. The 50%-shot row is not a universal winner against specialist phase models: it beats the specialist Jaccard values on AutoLaparo and M2CAI16, but on Cholec80 it trails Trans-SVNet in Jaccard, 78.7 versus 79.3, and trails LoViT in both accuracy and Jaccard, 92.1/78.7 versus 92.4/81.2.

3. Dataset: the scale comes from a strict funnel

The size table is the starting point. LEMON is larger than the classic single-procedure benchmarks and larger than the two other web-sourced surgical datasets shown by the paper. The important difference is not only the 85M-frame count. It is the jump from one-procedure datasets to a 35-procedure corpus.

The paper starts from about 18,000 raw videos. Video classification keeps 6,617. Subsequent trimming and preprocessing remove 66 hours of non-surgical footage, leaving 4,194 curated videos. The authors report that 94% of the final dataset is 1280 x 720 resolution, while the remaining 6% has varied resolutions, with the smallest at 640 x 480.

Each video receives two labels. Procedure type is multi-label over 35 classes. Surgery type is binary, robotic versus non-robotic. For label reliability, two researchers independently annotated a stratified random subset of 500 videos, more than 10% of the dataset. Agreement was Cohen's kappa 0.97 for procedure type and above 0.99 for surgery type. The manual workload was 106 person-hours, with the largest block, 72 hours, spent reviewing videos to remove out-of-body views and other artifacts.

4. Pipeline: a video-to-frame-to-region filter

The curation pipeline is a funnel. It uses cheap video-level screening first, then spends finer classifiers on the smaller set that remains. That order matters because the raw source contains long videos, and running precise frame and region models over everything would waste compute on obvious non-surgical material.

Q: Why not train directly on every frame after a keyword search?

A: A keyword search gives high recall but low precision. It finds medical videos, not necessarily intraoperative endoscopic content. The storyboard stage removes whole irrelevant videos. The frame stage removes intro and closing material. The region stage removes UI elements and logos that remain inside surgical frames. Each stage removes a different kind of shortcut.

Takeaway: The pipeline is designed around where the noise lives: video, frame, and region.

The reported curation validation is high-precision by design. The storyboard classifier uses 2,160 surgical and 1,910 non-surgical annotated storyboards and reaches 94.75% average F1 across five folds. The frame classifier uses 7,967 annotated frames, 5,481 surgical and 2,486 non-surgical, and reaches 95.64% average F1. The region detector uses 2,719 surgical frames with 4,584 non-surgical bounding boxes; its five-fold average is 79.29 mAP50 and 66.18 mAP50-95. During final inference, the paper uses five-model ensembles, a 70% confidence threshold, majority voting for classification, and non-maximum suppression for detection. Final review reports 100% precision at video level and above 99.9% precision at frame level.

5. Method: augmented DINO distillation for surgical invariance

LemonFM is an image foundation model trained from frames sampled at 1 fps from LEMON. The backbone is ConvNeXt-L. The learning scheme is based on DINO, a self-supervised knowledge-distillation method in which a student network learns to match a teacher network's soft output distribution across augmented views. The student parameters \(\theta_s\) are updated by gradient descent. The teacher parameters \(\theta_t\) are updated as an exponential moving average of the student.

The teacher and student map an image \(x\in\mathbb{R}^{H\times W\times 3}\) to a \(C\)-dimensional vector with \(C=2^{16}\). A softmax turns those vectors into categorical distributions \(P_t(z\mid x)=\sigma_z(f_{\theta_t}(x))\) and \(P_s(z\mid x)=\sigma_z(f_{\theta_s}(x))\), where \(z\in\{1,\ldots,C\}\) and \(\sigma_z\) is the \(z\)-th softmax output.

$$ \mathcal{L}=-\sum_{i=1}^{I}\sum_{u\in U_i}\sum_{\substack{v\in V_i\cup W_i\\u\neq v}}\sum_{z=1}^{C}P_t(z\mid u)\log P_s(z\mid v) $$
Eq. 1: \(I\) is the number of images. \(U_i\) contains two global augmentations of \(x_i\). \(V_i\) contains two global augmentations and four crops of \(x_i\). \(W_i\) is the paper's additional surgical supervision set.

How to read the loss

The teacher sees \(u\), a global view. The student sees \(v\), another view drawn from the usual same-frame views \(V_i\) or from the new set \(W_i\). The cross-entropy term asks the student distribution to match the teacher distribution. In vanilla DINO, this mainly teaches crop and color invariance. LemonFM changes the source of valid views, so the same distillation objective now also teaches invariance across nearby surgical motion and similar appearances in other patients.

$$ d_{\cos}(n,x_i)\lt 3\,d_{\cos}(x_{i,t-1},x_i) $$
Neighbor gate: a same-procedure frame \(n\) from another video is accepted only if it is close enough to the input \(x_i\), relative to the motion scale between adjacent frames in the input video.

Q: What assumption makes these extra positives reasonable?

Guide: a positive view is not just another image. It tells the model what should stay constant.

A: The method assumes two surgical invariances. Adjacent frames should keep the same tools and tissue layout despite small motion. Same-procedure frames from different patients can preserve procedure-level appearance even when color, anatomy, and camera pose change. The three-times distance gate is the quality control step: it tries to admit cross-patient views only when they are no farther from the input than a loose multiple of a normal adjacent-frame change.

Takeaway: \(W_i\) is the mechanism that turns generic DINO crop invariance into surgical invariance.

The supplement checks two design choices. Cosine distance beats L2 distance in k-nearest-neighbor phase evaluation, with AutoLaparo accuracy improving from 71.3 to 75.3 and Cholec80 from 66.8 to 70.3. The three-times threshold is near the best of the tested values. With ConvNeXt-S, AutoLaparo Acc/F1 is 72.9/63.2 at 1.5x, 73.4/63.7 at 3x, and 72.6/63.0 at 6x; Cholec80 Acc/F1 is 72.2/65.2, 73.6/66.7, and 72.7/65.2.

7. Experiments: when the representation is frozen and when it is tuned

The paper evaluates four task families. Phase recognition predicts the surgical phase of each frame or video segment; it reports accuracy and F1 in linear probing, and video-level accuracy plus Jaccard in full fine tuning. F1 is the harmonic mean of precision and recall. Jaccard is intersection over union for predicted and true phase segments. Tool presence detection and action recognition are multi-label frame tasks and use mean Average Precision, or mAP, where higher is better. Semantic segmentation uses mDice, the mean Dice coefficient, an overlap score where higher is better.

Q: Why would ConvNeXt matter more for segmentation than for phase recognition?

A: The paper's explanation is that ConvNeXt keeps a hierarchical convolutional structure with local connectivity. That bias helps preserve small surgical details such as tool tips. ViT patchification can collapse fine structure early. Phase recognition is a coarser video-state task, so both backbones can capture enough semantic context. Segmentation is a pixel-level task, so losing fine structure is costly.

Takeaway: The ablation supports two independent choices: discriminative distillation for transferable semantics, and a convolutional hierarchy for dense surgical detail.

Q: What is the most likely overstatement of this paper?

A: "Public YouTube data is enough for surgical foundation models." The paper shows a narrower and more useful claim: public video can work when it is curated aggressively, sampled at 1 fps, labeled at video level, filtered at video/frame/region levels, and paired with a distillation objective that encodes surgical invariances.

Takeaway: The success belongs to the pipeline plus the objective, not to raw web scale alone.

10. Summary: the useful mental model

In one sentence: LEMON turns public endoscopic video into a curated pretraining corpus, and LemonFM turns DINO's same-image invariance into surgical invariance by adding same-procedure neighbors and adjacent frames as positives.

Three takeaways:
  1. Data scale matters only after curation. The paper's funnel removes non-surgical videos, segments, frames, and regions before pretraining.
  2. The method's key move is \(W_i\). It tells the model which cross-patient and temporal changes should not alter the surgical representation.
  3. The evidence is strongest when separated by protocol: frozen linear probing, full fine tuning, segmentation, and the 50%-shot setting each make a different claim.
