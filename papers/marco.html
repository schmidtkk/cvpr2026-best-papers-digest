MARCO: what if sparse keypoint training is what breaks semantic correspondence?

Paper2Html2026-06-13CVPR 2026 digest entry · Politecnico di Torino / TU Darmstadt / hessian.AI / ELIZASource: arXiv:2604.18267v1 PDF

Root question. Semantic correspondence asks a model to match the same semantic part across two images, such as a chair leg in one view and the same leg in another. The field has leaned on dual encoders, pairing DINOv2, a self-supervised vision backbone, with Stable Diffusion features for local detail. Those models are accurate, but the paper argues that they are heavy and still brittle once the queried part was not annotated during training. MARCO asks whether the missing ingredient is not another backbone, but a denser training signal. It keeps one frozen DINOv2 ViT-L/14 backbone, adds lightweight adapters and a 4x feature upsampler, and trains with two objectives: a coarse-to-fine keypoint loss for precision and a dense self-distillation loss for generalization. On Percentage of Correct Keypoints (PCK) — the share of predicted keypoints landing within 10% of the object size of the truth, so higher is better — MARCO reaches 86.7 PCK@0.10 on SPair-71k (87.2 with the object-mask training setup that several baselines also use) and 67.5 on the unseen-keypoint split SPair-U, while using 323M parameters instead of the 950M of the dual-encoder Geo-SC and Jamais Vu. The useful idea is simple: if sparse landmark labels make the representation collapse around labeled points, mine reliable matches from the model itself and spread them over the object surface.

Q: What should the reader look for before trusting the headline?

Guide: separate three claims that can be confused: in-domain accuracy on standard benchmarks, transfer to unseen keypoints, and transfer to unseen object categories.

A: On the aggregate metric MARCO leads all three: 86.7/87.2 on seen keypoints (SPair-71k), 67.5 average on unseen keypoints (SPair-U), and best in all five MP-100 domains. Watch the per-class SPair-U columns, though — MARCO leads on the mean but loses several individual classes, so "wins" here means the average, not a clean sweep.

Takeaway: The paper is strongest where a single DINOv2 model stays accurate after the query moves outside the sparse training keypoint vocabulary.

Task Pixel-level semantic keypoint matching between image pairs.
Mechanism Coarse-to-fine Gaussian supervision plus flow-anchored dense self-distillation.
Claim to verify Better precision and better unseen-keypoint/category generalization, not one traded for the other.
Main caveat Sparse ground-truth keypoints are still needed as anchors during training.

1. Motivation: sparse supervision can erase useful structure

Semantic correspondence has a built-in supervision mismatch. The desired output is dense: any pixel on the source object may be queried. The available labels are sparse: SPair-71k provides 20 keypoints per image pair, and AP-10K provides 17 animal keypoints shared across 54 species. A model trained only on those locations can become very good at those locations while forgetting how the rest of the object should move.

The paper's visual evidence is the flow field around a pair of objects. Frozen DINOv2 already gives a partially coherent dense flow across the object surface. Fine-tuning with sparse keypoints improves landmarks but contracts the flow around the annotated points. MARCO's dense self-distillation is designed to push back against that contraction.

Q: Why can a model get better on annotated landmarks and worse on new landmarks?

Guide: follow where the gradients are. Sparse labels supervise a handful of positions; everything between them is trained only indirectly.

A: The model learns the labeled points first. If nothing protects the rest of the surface, the representation narrows around those points.

Takeaway: The core problem is not too little DINOv2 semantics. It is sparse training pressure that over-specializes those semantics.

2. Contributions: one model, one training principle, one harder test

The authors state three contributions. First, MARCO is a unified correspondence model that uses one DINOv2 backbone and still reaches the best reported scores in the paper's standard benchmark table. Second, the paper turns sparse keypoint supervision into dense pseudo-correspondences by mining reliable matches from DINOv2 feature space, densifying them with a piecewise-affine warp, and keeping only flow clusters that agree with ground-truth anchors. Third, it builds an MP-100 evaluation protocol that tests unseen keypoints inside familiar categories and unseen categories outside the SPair-71k taxonomy.

The mechanism hypothesis is precise enough to falsify. If dense self-distillation is the real cause, then removing it should hurt unseen-keypoint transfer more than seen-keypoint accuracy. If the pseudo-labeling pipeline is useful rather than decorative, each filtering and densification step should move the SPair-U result in the predicted direction. The paper checks both in Table 4 and adds a broader applicability check by applying the dense self-distillation loss to Geo-SC in the supplement.

Q: What is new if DINOv2 was already known to produce semantic matches?

Guide: distinguish the feature source from the training signal. DINOv2 supplies a useful feature space, but MARCO changes how that space is adapted.

A: The novelty is the training loop. MARCO mines reliable DINOv2 matches, spreads them over the surface, and uses sparse labels only to reject bad flow clusters.

Takeaway: MARCO is less a new matching backbone than a way to stop sparse supervision from destroying a useful pretrained geometry.

3. Datasets: in-domain accuracy is not the same as generalization

The paper trains and evaluates on several correspondence benchmarks. Standard benchmarks ask whether the model matches known landmark vocabularies well. SPair-U and MP-100 ask a harder question: does the model transfer to landmarks or categories not present during SPair-71k training?

Metric. Percentage of Correct Keypoints (PCK@alpha) counts a prediction as correct if it falls within \(\alpha\cdot\max(h,w)\) of the ground-truth point, where \(h\) and \(w\) are the object bounding-box height and width. Lower alpha is stricter. PCK@0.01 asks for fine localization, while PCK@0.10 is a coarser semantic match.

Q: Why does the page keep separating seen keypoints, unseen keypoints, and unseen categories?

Guide: a model can memorize a keypoint vocabulary, transfer to new keypoints in known categories, or transfer to new categories. Those are different tests.

A: Standard PCK does not prove open-ended correspondence. SPair-U and MP-100 test what happens after the query leaves the training labels.

Takeaway: MARCO's generalization claim lives or dies on SPair-U and MP-100, not only on SPair-71k.

4. Pipeline: freeze the backbone, then densify the supervision

MARCO keeps DINOv2 as the semantic backbone and learns only lightweight additions. AdaptFormer modules are inserted into higher transformer layers to adapt high-level features without full fine-tuning. A compact upsampling head raises the DINOv2 feature resolution by 4x so that strict thresholds such as PCK@0.01 are not capped by the original patch grid.

Q: Why freeze most of DINOv2 instead of fully fine-tuning it for the task?

Guide: the paper's appendix compares full fine-tuning with parameter-efficient adaptation. Full fine-tuning raises some in-domain performance but damages unseen-keypoint transfer.

A: The pretrained backbone already contains transferable structure. MARCO adjusts it lightly so the structure is not overwritten.

Takeaway: The architecture is conservative on purpose: learn enough for localization, but not so much that DINOv2 forgets its broad visual prior.

5. Method: make the target sharp, then make the labels dense

5.1 Lightweight adaptation and sub-patch features

Each AdaptFormer module applies a token-wise bottleneck and adds it back through a residual path. The backbone weights are frozen, so the learned matrices refine high-level features without rewriting the whole encoder.

$$A(x)=\mathrm{GELU}(xW_{\mathrm{down}})W_{\mathrm{up}},\qquad x'=\mathrm{MLP}(x_{\mathrm{self}})+x_{\mathrm{self}}+A(x_{\mathrm{self}}),\qquad d\ll D$$
Eq. 1: adapter update from the paper's Eq. 1-3. \(D\) is the embedding dimension and \(d\) is the bottleneck dimension.

The upsampling head then increases feature resolution. This matters because a 14x14 patch token is too coarse when the metric threshold is only 1 percent of the object box.

$$F_1=\mathrm{ConvTranspose}_{2\times2}(F),\qquad \hat F=\mathrm{DepthwiseConv}_{3\times3}\big(\mathrm{GELU}(F_1)\big)$$
Eq. 2: feature upsampling from the paper's Eq. 4-5.

How to read Eq. 1-2

The adapter changes the descriptor content. The upsampler changes the descriptor grid. Together they make DINOv2 both more correspondence-aware and more spatially precise, without adding a second encoder.

5.2 Coarse-to-fine supervised matching

For a source keypoint \(p_i^s\), MARCO compares its descriptor to every target-grid descriptor and gets a similarity map. Instead of regressing one coordinate with soft-argmax and an L2 loss, it supervises the whole map against a Gaussian radial basis function target centered on the ground-truth target keypoint \(p_i^t\).

$$S(p_i^s,u)=\langle \hat F_s[p_i^s],\hat F_t[u]\rangle,\qquad G_{\sigma}(u;p_i^t)\propto \exp\!\left(-\frac{\lVert u-p_i^t\rVert_2^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$
Eq. 3: similarity map and Gaussian target from the paper's Eq. 6-7.
$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{sup}}=-\frac{1}{K}\sum_{i=1}^{K}\sum_{u\in\hat\Lambda}G_{\sigma}(u;p_i^t)\log \mathrm{softmax}\,S(p_i^s,u)$$
Eq. 4: distributional cross-entropy supervision from the paper's Eq. 8.

The key variable is the bandwidth \(\sigma\). A wide Gaussian teaches rough semantic parts. A narrow Gaussian teaches precise localization. MARCO anneals from wide to narrow with a cosine schedule:

$$\sigma(t)=\sigma_{\min}+\frac{1}{2}(\sigma_{\max}-\sigma_{\min})\left(1+\cos\frac{\pi t}{T}\right),\qquad \sigma_{\max}=3,\quad \sigma_{\min}=1$$
Eq. 5: coarse-to-fine bandwidth schedule from the paper's Eq. 9 and implementation details.

5.3 Dense self-distillation via flow anchoring

The second objective builds pseudo-labels over the object surface. The exponential moving average teacher first finds mutual nearest-neighbor matches between source and target features:

$$\mathrm{NN}_{s\to t}(u)=\arg\max_{v\in\hat\Lambda}\langle F_s^T[u],F_t^T[v]\rangle,\qquad \mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{MNN}}=\{(u,v)\mid \mathrm{NN}_{s\to t}(u)=v\wedge \mathrm{NN}_{t\to s}(v)=u\}$$
Eq. 6: mutual nearest-neighbor seeds from the paper's Eq. 10-11.

Those seeds are merged with sparse ground-truth correspondences. A Delaunay triangulation on the source seed points defines local triangles. Replacing each triangle's vertices with their target matches gives a piecewise-affine warp \(\hat W\), and the dense displacement is:

$$u\mapsto D(u)=\hat W(u)-u$$
Eq. 7: dense displacement field from the paper's Eq. 12.

Dense flow alone is not enough, because symmetric parts can move coherently to the wrong target region. MARCO clusters displacement vectors, forms source and target regions for each cluster, and keeps only clusters whose flow is anchored by a ground-truth correspondence:

$$C_n^s=\{u\mid D(u)\in\Omega_n\},\qquad C_n^t=\{u+D(u)\mid u\in C_n^s\}$$
Eq. 8: source and target regions induced by one flow cluster, from the paper's Eq. 13-14.
$$\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{self}}=\{(u,u+D(u))\mid \exists n,\,(p_i^s,p_i^t)\in E:\ u\in C_n^s\wedge p_i^s\in C_n^s\wedge p_i^t\in C_n^t\}$$
Eq. 9: ground-truth-anchored pseudo-label set from the paper's Eq. 15.

The student then regresses these pseudo-correspondences, while the teacher follows the student by exponential moving average:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{self}}=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{self}}|}\sum_{(\hat u,\hat v)\in\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{self}}}\left\lVert \operatorname{softargmax}_{u\in\hat\Lambda}S(\hat u,u)-\hat v\right\rVert_2^2,\qquad \theta_T\leftarrow \beta\theta_T+(1-\beta)\theta_S$$
Eq. 10: self-distillation loss and EMA teacher update from the paper's Eq. 16-17.

Why CE for ground truth but L2 for pseudo-labels?

Ground-truth keypoints are trusted, so a sharp distributional target is useful. Pseudo-labels are noisy, so a softer regression target is safer. The loss form follows the reliability of the supervision.

Q: What closes the gap between sparse keypoint labels and dense pixel queries?

Guide: look at the role of the ground-truth keypoints. They are not used to label every pixel; they decide which mined flow regions are trustworthy.

A: The model mines many candidate matches, spreads them across triangles, and keeps the regions that agree with sparse anchors.

Takeaway: Sparse labels become a quality filter for dense pseudo-labels, not the whole supervision signal.

6. Training and compute: what the paper discloses

The implementation details are compact. MARCO uses DINOv2 ViT-L/14, trains the upsampling head and AdaptFormer blocks in the last 12 transformer layers, and keeps the main backbone frozen. The optimizer is Adam with learning rate \(1\times10^{-4}\). The teacher exponential moving average momentum is \(\beta=0.999\). Training runs for 10 epochs with batch size 16. Unless stated otherwise, candidate pixels are restricted to SAM-derived object masks during training; at inference the method uses Window soft-argmax as in Jamais Vu and Geo-SC.

Q: What would be the easiest overstatement?

Guide: compare "dense self-distillation helps generalization" with "the method solves semantic correspondence in general."

A: The paper supports the first claim, not the second. It still needs sparse anchors and DINOv2-quality seeds.

Takeaway: MARCO is best read as a strong sparse-supervision amplifier, not as the end of correspondence supervision.

10. Summary: the useful mental model

In one sentence: MARCO treats sparse keypoint labels as anchors for selecting reliable dense pseudo-correspondences, so a single DINOv2 backbone can improve both fine localization and transfer beyond the seen keypoint vocabulary.

Three takeaways

  1. The failure is targeted. Sparse supervised fine-tuning can make seen keypoints better while damaging object-wide flow.
  2. The fix is dense but filtered. MARCO mines mutual nearest neighbors, spreads them with Delaunay warps, and keeps only ground-truth-anchored flow clusters.
  3. The evidence comes from multiple angles. Figure 2 shows the flow restoration, Table 4 shows the causal ablation, Table 2 and Table 3 show zero-shot generalization, and Supplementary Table 5 shows the dense loss helps another model too.

Q: What is the smallest version of the paper's idea?

Guide: remove the engineering details and keep the causal claim.

A: Do not let sparse labels be the whole teacher. Use them to validate dense labels the model can mine for itself.

Takeaway: The paper's design pattern is sparse labels as anchors, dense self-supervision as coverage.
