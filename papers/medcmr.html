Med-CMR: what breaks when medical VLMs must see, reason, and generalize at once?

Medical AIBenchmarkMLLM EvaluationSeven-Dimension DecompositionLLM-as-JudgeLong-Tail GeneralizationMathJax checked

The short version. Med-CMR asks a sharper question than ordinary medical visual question answering: can a multimodal large language model, or MLLM, use medical images and clinical context together when the answer requires small visual evidence, multiple views, time, causality, or rare disease knowledge? The paper turns that question into a benchmark with 20,653 visual question answering pairs, split into 16,655 five-option multiple-choice questions (MCQs) and 3,998 open-ended questions. Its core design move is a seven-way capability split: three visual dimensions and four reasoning dimensions, so a model can fail in a diagnosable way instead of only losing one overall score. The most defensible headline is not that any model is clinically ready; it is that even the best evaluated closed model, GPT-5, reaches only 57.81 MCQ accuracy and 48.70 open-ended score on this stress test. Gemini-2.5-Pro follows at 49.87 MCQ and 45.98 open-ended, while the best open-source MCQ result is Qwen3-VL-235B-A22B at 49.34 and the best open-source open-ended result is InternVL3.5-241B-A28B at 47.88. Long-tail generalization is the hardest MCQ dimension: the top score is GPT-5's 55.19, and every open-source model is below 46. The paper also finds that medically fine-tuned models do not reliably beat their general base models on MCQ reasoning, even though they can produce more medical-looking open-ended text. Read Med-CMR as a benchmark for locating failure modes, not as a proof that benchmark scores are clinical validation.

1. Motivation: why does one medical VQA score hide the real problem?

Most medical VQA benchmarks ask whether a model can describe an image or retrieve a clear fact from a short context. That is useful, but it misses the cases where clinical reasoning becomes fragile. A small bleeding source can be visually subtle. A temporal sequence can change the diagnosis. A rare staining pattern can require knowledge that is not common in training data. A model that answers easy radiology questions may still fail when it must connect image evidence to clinical logic.

Med-CMR treats that mismatch as the central problem. Instead of asking whether an MLLM "understands medicine" as a single property, the benchmark asks which part fails. Did the model miss a small object? Did it confuse similar-looking findings? Did it use only one image when the case required multiple sources? Did it know the common disease but not the rare mechanism? Those are different failures, and they need different measurements.

Q: Why does the benchmark need both visual and reasoning dimensions?

Guide: a correct medical answer often depends on a visual observation and an inference over that observation.

A: Because a model can fail before or after reasoning begins. It may never see the tiny lesion, so the reasoning has no evidence. Or it may see the finding but connect it to the wrong mechanism. Med-CMR splits the problem so those two errors do not collapse into the same wrong-answer count.

Takeaway: The benchmark's value is diagnostic. It makes the failure mode visible.

2. Contributions: what is new here?

The authors state three main contributions. First, Med-CMR is a fine-grained benchmark for clinical complex reasoning in MLLMs, organized around seven capability dimensions. Second, it uses a two-track evaluation protocol: MCQ accuracy for answer correctness and an open-ended score for reasoning and generation quality. Third, it benchmarks 18 proprietary and open-source MLLMs, then analyzes why strong general models can outperform medical-domain models on complex reasoning.

The novelty is the decomposition. Earlier medical VQA datasets can have broad image coverage or many questions, but they usually do not isolate the kind of clinical complexity being tested. Med-CMR separates the question type from the organ system and modality, so a low score on long-tail generalization means something different from a low score on spatial understanding.

3. Data: seven dimensions, real cases, and two-stage review

The paper builds Med-CMR from authentic journal case reports and clinical research articles, including sources such as JMCR and NEJM. The collected material includes medical images, human-annotated captions, and metadata. Annotators with medical backgrounds design 10 to 20 templates for each question category, and GPT-5-mini-2025-08-07 helps instantiate the templates by selecting a suitable template and extracting the correct answer from the human caption.

For MCQ distractors, three models generate candidate wrong answers: GPT-5-Mini-2025-08-07, Qwen3-VL-Plus-2025-09-23, and Claude-Sonnet-4-20250514. Each model proposes four distractors, producing twelve candidates. Three medical-background annotators select the final four distractors under four constraints: the option must be difficult, false, visually dependent, and clinically plausible.

Quality control has two filters. Human filtering removes images with insufficient captions or mismatches to the target dimension. Model filtering removes questions that Lingshu-7B, Qwen2.5-VL-7B, and Llava-Med-v1.5-Mistral-7B all answer correctly, so the remaining questions are less likely to be trivial. General practitioners help screen generated content, leading to removal of 8% of questionable synthetic items, and licensed physicians perform the final medical-accuracy review.

Q: Is the benchmark synthetic because models help write questions?

A: The questions are model-assisted, but the image evidence and captions come from real medical articles, and the final items pass human and physician review. The more precise concern is not "synthetic or real"; it is whether the model-written wording and distractors remain clinically faithful. That is why the paper's multi-stage review is a central part of the benchmark, not an implementation detail.

Takeaway: Med-CMR is best understood as real-case evidence wrapped in templated, reviewed questions.

4. Pipeline: how a case report becomes a benchmark item

Med-CMR's pipeline is a curation and evaluation pipeline, not a model-training pipeline. It starts with dimension-guided data collection, turns cases into MCQ and open-ended VQA items, filters out low-quality or easy questions, then scores models under a unified protocol.

The important design choice is the model-filtering step. A benchmark that includes too many questions solved by small or older models will mostly measure easy recognition. Med-CMR removes questions that all three screening models answer correctly, which shifts the set toward cases that expose current failures. That also means the absolute scores should not be compared directly to easier medical VQA benchmarks.

5. Method: MCQ accuracy plus an evidence-weighted open-ended score

The evaluation has two tracks. MCQs are scored by correctness after answer extraction with regular expressions. Open-ended responses are judged by DeepSeek-V3.2-Exp under a fixed rubric and normalized to a 0-100 scale in the experiments. The four open-ended dimensions are consistency, coherence, visual accuracy, and ground-truth correctness. Consistency means clear, internally non-contradictory expression. Coherence means the reasoning chain is logically connected. Visual accuracy means the model's described visual features match the image caption. Ground-truth correctness means the reasoning and final answer match the expert reference.

$$ S=\frac{\sum_{i\in\{\mathrm{cons},\mathrm{coh},\mathrm{vis},\mathrm{gt}\}} w_i s_i}{\sum_{i\in\{\mathrm{cons},\mathrm{coh},\mathrm{vis},\mathrm{gt}\}} w_i} $$
Eq. 1: open-ended weighted score from paper Section 2.4.

How to read Eq. 1

The score \(s_i\) is the judge score for one dimension, and \(w_i\) is its weight. The paper sets \(w_{\mathrm{cons}}=1\), \(w_{\mathrm{coh}}=1\), \(w_{\mathrm{vis}}=4\), and \(w_{\mathrm{gt}}=4\). The formula therefore makes visual evidence and ground-truth correctness four times as important as language clarity or reasoning coherence.

This weighting is the benchmark's most important methodological choice. It prevents a fluent answer from dominating the open-ended score when the visual evidence is wrong. That matters because the result table shows near-ceiling consistency for many models, while visual accuracy and ground-truth correctness remain much lower.

Q: Why not let open-ended scoring be mostly about the final answer?

A: Because the benchmark is about complex medical reasoning, not answer-string matching. A model can output a plausible final term while inventing a visual feature. By weighting visual accuracy and ground-truth correctness at 4 each, Med-CMR makes unsupported reasoning expensive.

Takeaway: The open-ended score is designed to punish fluent but visually ungrounded answers.

6. Evaluation setup: no new model training in the benchmark

Med-CMR itself is an evaluation benchmark. The paper does not train a new model for the benchmark results. It evaluates 18 MLLMs: GPT-5 and Gemini-2.5-Pro as closed-source systems, plus 16 open-source systems from the Gemma3, Medgemma, Lingshu, Qwen2.5-VL, InternVL3.5, and Qwen3-VL families.

Open-source models are deployed with vLLM. Closed-source models are accessed through official APIs. The paper constrains output format with prompts and extracts MCQ answers with regular-expression matching. It does not disclose a full GPU count, wall-clock time, or cost for running the evaluation. Because this is pure evaluation rather than training a new model, a GPU-hours training estimate is not applicable.

8. Analyses: do the failure modes explain the numbers?

This paper does not run ablations in the sense of removing layers from a new model. Its ablation-like evidence is analysis: error typing, base-versus-medical model comparison, reformulated MCQs, and human alignment for the LLM judge.

8.1 GPT-5 failure analysis

The authors treat GPT-5 as the current upper-bound model and sample 100 error cases for each of the seven dimensions. They manually categorize failures into recognition errors, reasoning errors, medical-knowledge errors, question-understanding errors, and format errors. The paper reports that recognition, reasoning, and insufficient medical knowledge dominate, while question misunderstanding and format issues are rare.

The observations are clinically intuitive. In visually demanding categories, GPT-5 can rely on global appearance and miss subtle evidence: small features, overlapping structures, or inconsistent slice-level tracking. In tasks such as spatial understanding, multi-source integration, temporal prediction, and causal reasoning, the model can identify some evidence but fail to connect it across views, time, or context. In long-tail cases, temporal prediction, and causal reasoning, the model still needs more specialized medical knowledge than the benchmarked systems reliably provide.

8.2 Medical fine-tuning is not a guaranteed reasoning upgrade

Figure 4(b) reports a consistent pattern: medically fine-tuned models decline on MCQ tasks relative to their base models, while the open-ended gap narrows and sometimes reverses. The Table 2 pairs make it concrete: on MCQ All, Gemma3-27B (37.07) drops to its medical fine-tune Medgemma-27B (28.91), and Qwen2.5-VL-32B (33.36) drops to Lingshu-32B (30.47). On Open All the picture is mixed — Lingshu-32B reverses it (43.02 vs 41.22), but Medgemma-27B still trails (31.49 vs 35.36). The authors investigate this by manually reviewing 150 MCQs that Qwen2.5-VL-32B answered correctly but Lingshu-32B missed, another 150 that Gemma-27B answered correctly but Medgemma-27B missed, and 50 open-ended questions where Lingshu-32B scored higher than Qwen2.5-VL-32B.

The proposed explanation is a trade-off. Medical fine-tuning can add richer domain semantics and more medically aligned language, which helps open-ended responses look and sound more clinical. But it can also weaken general multimodal reasoning: the model follows familiar medical patterns, misses subtle visual or contextual cues, and jumps to prototypical diagnoses. That is why the page should not phrase "medical model" as automatically better on Med-CMR.

8.3 The LLM judge is checked, but still a proxy

For open-ended scoring, the paper validates DeepSeek-V3.2-Exp against human rankings. It samples 200 open-ended VQA items and collects answers from Qwen3-8B, Qwen3-32B, and MedGemma-27B. Two annotators independently rank responses on consistency, coherence, visual accuracy, and ground-truth correctness, then reconcile disagreements. The reported maximum win-ratio difference between human and LLM evaluation is 0.0449, occurring in the coherence dimension for MedGemma-27B. Spearman's \(\rho\) exceeds 0.8 for consistency and visual accuracy, and 0.78 for coherence and ground-truth correctness.

Q: What do the analyses add beyond Table 2?

A: They explain why the table should not be read as simple scaling wins or simple medical-domain wins. Scaling helps MCQ accuracy, but visual evidence remains hard in open-ended scoring. Medical fine-tuning improves some medical semantics, but it can reduce fine-grained multimodal reasoning. The strongest systems still fail by missing evidence, connecting it poorly, or lacking rare-case knowledge.

Takeaway: Med-CMR's key result is the shape of the failure, not just the ordering of models.

Q: What is the most likely overstatement?

A: "GPT-5 solves medical multimodal reasoning." The paper's own strongest number is 57.81 MCQ accuracy, and long-tail generalization peaks at 55.19. A more faithful claim is that GPT-5 is the best evaluated closed model on Med-CMR, while the benchmark still exposes large gaps in rare-case and visually grounded reasoning.

Takeaway: The benchmark is useful precisely because the best models still look weak under it.

10. Summary: the useful mental model

In one sentence: Med-CMR turns medical multimodal reasoning from a single score into a seven-axis stress test, then shows that current MLLMs still struggle most when rare medical knowledge must be grounded in subtle image evidence.

Three takeaways

  1. The seven-way split is the contribution. It separates missed evidence from weak reasoning, and separates common-case recognition from long-tail medical generalization.
  2. The headline results need scopes attached. GPT-5 leads the evaluated closed-source models at 57.81 MCQ and 48.70 open-ended score; Qwen3-VL-235B-A22B is the best open-source MCQ model at 49.34; InternVL3.5-241B-A28B is the best open-source open-ended model at 47.88.
  3. Fluency is not enough. Consistency scores are high, but visual accuracy and ground-truth correctness remain low, which is why the open-ended score weights them four times more heavily.

The practical reading path is: start with Figure 1 to understand the seven axes, read Eq. 1 to see why visual evidence matters in open-ended scoring, use Table 2 for the main model comparison, then read the analyses to understand why scaling and medical fine-tuning do not solve the same failure.
