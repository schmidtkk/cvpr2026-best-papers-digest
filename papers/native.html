Native and Compact Structured Latents for 3D Generation

Paper2Html deep-read2026-06-26Tsinghua University · Microsoft Research · USTC · Microsoft AISource: arXiv:2512.14692v1 PDF
3D GenerationO-VoxelSparse Compression VAEPBR MaterialsFlow MatchingMathJax checked

The short version. The paper asks a practical 3D-generation question: can a model generate full assets if its latent, the compressed code a generator actually models, is both native to 3D and small enough to scale? Most recent systems compress shape through field-style surfaces or through SLAT, a structured 3D latent from TRELLIS built from multiview image features, and that makes open surfaces, enclosed details, and material attributes hard to keep together. Native and Compact Structured Latents for 3D Generation introduces O-Voxel, a sparse voxel representation that stores geometry and appearance directly on active voxels instead of first converting the asset into a scalar field or a set of rendered views. Each active voxel stores shape plus physically based rendering (PBR) material values: base color, metallic, roughness, and opacity. A Sparse Compression variational autoencoder (SC-VAE), meaning an encoder-decoder that learns a regularized compressed latent, downsamples this O-Voxel grid by $16\times$ and yields about $9.6\mathrm{K}$ latent tokens for a $1024^3$ asset. A roughly 4B-parameter flow-matching generator, a model trained to learn a vector field that moves noise toward data, then performs image-to-3D generation in that latent space. The measurements are strong but not one-dimensional: the $1024^3$ native model wins every Table 1 fidelity metric and every reported Table 2 metric, yet its $0.301$s decoder runtime is not the fastest Table 1 number. Read the paper as a representation paper first, with generation quality following from the representation being native, compact, and material-aware.

Root questionCan a 3D latent stay compact without throwing away open surfaces, enclosed structures, and material attributes?
Representation answerO-Voxel stores active voxels $f=\{(f_i^{\mathrm{shape}}, f_i^{\mathrm{mat}}, p_i)\}$ on an $N\times N\times N$ grid, with field-free shape and PBR material in the same sparse structure.
Compression answerSC-VAE uses sparse residual autoencoding to reach $16\times$ spatial downsampling, about $2.2\mathrm{K}$ tokens at $512^3$, $9.6\mathrm{K}$ at $1024^3$, and about $20\mathrm{K}$ at $1536^3$.
Generation answerThe image-to-3D generator reports about 3s at $512^3$, 17s at $1024^3$, and 60s at $1536^3$ on one NVIDIA H100.

1. Motivation: why are current 3D latents not enough?

Q: What breaks when a 3D generator compresses shape and appearance through the wrong representation?

Guide: separate three burdens that a production asset carries. It must represent arbitrary topology, including open surfaces and non-manifold geometry. It must preserve high-frequency shape, including enclosed internal surfaces. It must also carry material, not just color.

A: A field-based representation first asks the asset to behave like a scalar field such as a signed distance function. That makes extraction convenient, but it ties geometry to sign changes and iso-surfaces. Open sheets, self-intersections, and enclosed structures do not naturally fit that assumption. A multiview latent can add appearance, but it inherits the blind spots of the rendered views and must fuse view-dependent evidence back into 3D. The paper's move is to store the asset on the 3D grid where the surface actually lives.

Takeaway: The bottleneck is not only model size. It is whether the latent starts from a representation that can express the asset without a lossy detour.

A non-manifold surface is geometry whose local neighborhood is not a single clean sheet, for example a self-intersection or a place where several sheets meet. A field-free representation does not query a signed distance field or occupancy field and then detect sign changes. It uses the mesh surface directly. That difference is the paper's first premise: if the raw asset is a mesh with material maps, the latent should be learned from a structure that can round-trip that native asset without rendering, SDF sampling, flood fill, or iterative optimization.

The paper positions SLAT as the closest prior structured latent. SLAT is TRELLIS's sparse structured 3D latent built from multiview 2D information. It is effective for scalable generation, but the paper argues that its reliance on multiview features and rendering supervision makes complex structures and materials harder to capture. O-Voxel answers with a sparse grid whose active cells carry both geometry and material values.

2. Contribution: what is new, and what is just engineering?

Q: Is the paper's contribution a new generator, a new geometry representation, or a faster implementation?

Guide: follow the dependency chain. O-Voxel makes the representation native. SC-VAE makes it compact. The flow model and FlexGEMM make the representation usable at scale.

A: The representation is the load-bearing contribution. O-Voxel defines what information exists. SC-VAE compresses that information into a smaller latent space. The roughly 4B-parameter flow models show that the latent can drive image-to-3D generation, and the Triton sparse-convolution backend makes the sparse VAE practical.

Takeaway: The paper should be judged first by the representation and reconstruction numbers, then by generation quality.

The paper's stated contributions are four. First, it introduces O-Voxel, or Omni-Voxel, a sparse voxel structure that stores geometry and appearance over active voxels on an $N\times N\times N$ grid:

$$f=\{(f_i^{\mathrm{shape}}, f_i^{\mathrm{mat}}, p_i)\}_{i=1}^{L},\qquad p_i\in\{0,1,\ldots,N-1\}^3.$$
Eq. 1: an O-Voxel asset is a set of active voxel features, not a dense field.

Second, it defines a Flexible Dual Grid (FDG) geometry feature that can represent open, non-manifold, and fully enclosed surfaces. Third, it adds PBR material values to active voxels, including opacity for translucent surfaces. Fourth, it trains sparse compression VAEs and flow-matching generators on this representation, with a custom FlexGEMM backend for sparse convolution.

The wording matters. This page treats the paper as a curator-highlight paper in the digest. The PDF does not state any special venue label, so this page does not add one.

3. Datasets: what data actually supports the claims?

Q: Which assets train the representation, and which assets test whether it generalizes?

Guide: the paper uses different splits for SC-VAE training, generator training, reconstruction evaluation, and user studies. Do not collapse them into one number.

A: The SC-VAE is trained on a filtered public-asset collection with PBR materials. The generator uses a larger collection augmented with TexVerse. Reconstruction is evaluated on Toys4K-PBR and a 90-asset recent Sketchfab Featured test set. Generation and user studies use 100 AI-generated image prompts that are separate from training.

Takeaway: The evidence is broad for public object assets, but the paper does not claim coverage of every production domain or scene-level setting.

The appendix gives the clearest dataset accounting. The authors start from the TRELLIS data-preparation setup, exclude 3D-FUTURE because it lacks PBR materials, and parse raw assets with Blender to keep assets using a metallic-roughness PBR workflow. They also use an aesthetic-score filter and render 16 image views per asset with randomized field of view and lighting for image conditioning.

4. Pipeline: what flows from mesh to generation?

Q: Why is bidirectional conversion central instead of just a preprocessing detail?

Guide: a representation used for generation has to meet two constraints. It must be cheap to produce from real assets, and it must decode back into assets that tools can render.

A: O-Voxel is designed as a bridge. Mesh-to-O-Voxel takes seconds on a single CPU because it uses analytic mesh intersections and direct texture sampling. O-Voxel-to-surface-plus-material takes tens of milliseconds because the dual grid already contains the vertices, connection flags, and material values needed for reconstruction.

Takeaway: The representation is useful because it is both learnable by sparse neural networks and convertible back to standard 3D assets without optimization.

The shape path starts from mesh triangles. The algorithm finds grid edges intersected by the mesh, marks neighboring voxels active, and stores Hermite data. Hermite data means the intersection point $q_i$ and surface normal $n_i$ associated with an edge crossing. The material path samples texture maps at surface projections near each active voxel, averages PBR attributes by distance, and stores them as voxel material features. Decoding reverses the two steps: FDG connects dual vertices into faces, and trilinear interpolation assigns material values to mesh vertices or texture-map texels.

The generator then uses three flow models: one for sparse structure, one for geometry latents, and one for material latents conditioned on the generated shape. The reported inference speeds are about 3s for $512^3$, 17s for $1024^3$, and 60s for $1536^3$ fully textured assets on a single NVIDIA H100. The Table 1 decoder runtimes are a different measurement: they are SC-VAE decoder times on an NVIDIA A100.

5. Method: how does O-Voxel represent shape and material?

Q: What does "field-free dual contouring" mean here?

Guide: ordinary dual contouring starts from a signed grid and finds where signs change. This paper keeps the useful dual-grid idea but replaces sign changes with direct mesh-surface intersections.

A: The dual vertex still sits inside a voxel and is solved from local geometric evidence. But the evidence comes from the mesh itself: intersected grid edges, intersection points, normals, and boundary edges. That is why open surfaces can be represented. There is no need for an inside/outside sign to flip across the surface.

Takeaway: FDG keeps the sharp-feature behavior of dual contouring while removing the watertight-field assumption.

Dual contouring is a surface extraction method that places one vertex in each active cell and connects neighboring dual vertices to form faces. The paper's Flexible Dual Grid is a field-free variant. For each active voxel, the geometric feature contains three parts:

  • a dual vertex $v_i\in\mathbb{R}^3$, placed inside the grid cell;
  • edge-intersection flags $\delta_i\in\{0,1\}^3$, one for each of the three predefined voxel-edge directions used to decide which dual faces exist;
  • splitting weights $\gamma_i\in\mathbb{R}_{\gt 0}$, which control how quadrilateral faces split into triangles.

The dual vertex is solved in closed form by a quadratic error function (QEF), a least-squares objective that places the vertex close to the local surface planes, boundary edges, and a stabilizing reference point:

$$ \min_{v\in\mathrm{voxel}} e(v) =\sum_i d_{\Pi,i}^2+\lambda_{\mathrm{bound}}\sum_j d_{L,j}^2+\lambda_{\mathrm{reg}}d_{\hat q}^2. $$
Eq. 2: the FDG QEF used to place one dual vertex in an active voxel.
$$ d_{\Pi,i}^2=(n_i\cdot(v-q_i))^2,\qquad d_{L,j}^2=\left\lVert (v-o_j)-((v-o_j)\cdot d_j)d_j\right\rVert^2,\qquad d_{\hat q}^2=\left\lVert v-\bar q\right\rVert^2. $$
Eq. 3: plane distance, boundary-line distance, and regularization distance.

The first term is the classic dual-contouring term: it keeps $v$ near the planes implied by intersection points and normals. The boundary term is the open-surface fix. If a mesh sheet has a boundary edge, an SDF-style sign-change rule has no natural closed inside/outside volume to rely on; the boundary-line distance instead tells the vertex to align with that open edge. The regularization term keeps the solution near the average intersection point $\bar q$. Without it, sparse or nearly parallel local planes can make the least-squares system ill-conditioned, so small numerical perturbations would move the dual vertex too far.

Material is simpler but equally important. For each active voxel, the paper stores the PBR feature

$$ f_i^{\mathrm{mat}}=(c_i,m_i,r_i,\alpha_i), $$
Eq. 4: material feature with base color, metallic, roughness, and opacity.

Here $c_i\in\mathbb{R}^3$ is base color, $m_i$ is metallic ratio, $r_i$ is roughness, and $\alpha_i$ is opacity. Opacity is not cosmetic: it enables translucent surfaces such as glass-like parts, a case the paper calls out as missing from prior methods. The material values are aligned with geometry because they are stored at the same active voxels, not bolted on after shape generation.

6. Training: how is the compact latent learned?

Q: Why does $16\times$ spatial downsampling not destroy the asset?

Guide: sparse voxels are not dense images. Most of the grid is empty, and the useful information sits near active surfaces. The VAE must compress that sparse structure without losing local detail.

A: SC-VAE uses sparse residual autoencoding. During downsampling, each local group of child voxels is rearranged into channels and averaged into a coarse residual estimate. During upsampling, the coarse feature is redistributed back to space. This gives the model a shortcut for local structure, so the learned convolutional blocks do not have to reconstruct every fine detail from scratch.

Takeaway: The compactness is not just fewer tokens; it is a sparse architecture designed around where surface information lives.

The appendix gives the SC-VAE scale: about 800M parameters in total, with 354M in the encoder and 474M in the decoder. The model is fully sparse-convolutional, uses ConvNeXt-style residual blocks, and uses early-pruning during upsampling to skip inactive child voxels. The paper trains shape and material SC-VAEs separately so material generation can condition on a given shape.

Training happens in two stages. Stage 1 stabilizes low-resolution learning at $256^3$ with direct O-Voxel regression plus KL regularization. Stage 2 increases resolution to $512^3$ and adds rendering losses on masks, depth, normals, color, and the metallic-roughness-alpha map. The paper writes the second-stage loss as

$$ L_{s2}=L_{s1}+L_{\mathrm{render}}. $$
Eq. 5: the second stage keeps O-Voxel supervision and adds rendering-based perceptual supervision.

The flow generator is trained with rectified flow. It linearly interpolates between a data latent $x_0$ and noise $\epsilon$ as $x(t)=(1-t)x_0+t\epsilon$, then learns the vector field that points from data to noise:

$$ L_{\mathrm{CFM}}(\theta)=\mathbb{E}_{t,x_0,\epsilon} \left\lVert v_\theta(x(t),t)-(\epsilon-x_0)\right\rVert_2^2. $$
Eq. 6: conditional flow-matching objective used for the generative models.

The generator has three transformer-based DiT modules. Each contains about 1.3B parameters, with width 1536, 30 blocks, 12 attention heads, and MLP width 8192. The paper trains each DiT on 32 H100 GPUs with batch size 256. It uses DINOv3-L image features, AdaLN-single timestep conditioning, RoPE positional encoding, QK normalization, AdamW with learning rate $1\times 10^{-4}$, weight decay 0.01, and classifier-free guidance drop rate 0.1.

Guide: keep resolution, token count, metric, and runtime device separate. Table 1 compares reconstruction at listed resolutions and reports decoder runtime on A100. Table 2 compares image-to-3D generation on 100 prompts and mixes automatic metrics with a user study.

A: The strongest reconstruction comparison is Ours $1024^3$ versus TRELLIS at the same 9.6K token count, and versus SparseFlex $1024^3$ as the strongest high-token shape baseline. The strongest generation comparison is Ours versus Hunyuan3D 2.1 for metrics where Hunyuan reports PBR-capable output, and versus TRELLIS, Step1X-3D, Hi3DGen, and Direct3D-S2 where their metrics are reported.

8. Ablations: which design choices matter?

Q: Is SC-VAE's gain from the residual autoencoding shortcut, the optimized residual block, or just more capacity?

Guide: Table 3 removes one mechanism at a time at $256^3$ on curated Sketchfab assets. Compare MD, F1, PSNR, LPIPS, and decoder time under the same token budget.

A: Sparse residual autoencoding is the biggest load-bearing piece. Removing it at f16c32 raises MD from 1.032 to 1.747 and lowers PSNR from 27.26 to 26.73. Removing the optimized residual block also hurts, raising MD to 1.198 and lowering PSNR to 26.67. At the more compressed f32c128 setting, removing residual autoencoding is severe: MD rises from 1.405 to 7.394.

Takeaway: The compact latent works because the architecture preserves local sparse structure during downsampling, not because the model simply has many parameters.

The test-time scaling argument depends on compactness. After predicting a lower-resolution sparse structure, the generator can reapply the geometry stage on a denser sparse layout, for example moving from a $1024^3$ O-Voxel toward a $1536^3$ O-Voxel. The same cascaded idea can be used at $1024^3$ to clean local structure. The paper reports visual improvements but does not provide a separate numeric ablation table for this scaling figure.

9. Limitations: where can the representation fail?

Q: What does O-Voxel still not solve?

Guide: look for limits set by the grid, the sparse decoder, and the semantic level of the representation.

A: The grid bounds the smallest geometry it can represent. If two close parallel surfaces fall inside the same voxel, the QEF may place one dual vertex between them, and material values may average together. Sparse decoding can also leave small holes. Finally, the representation stores geometry and material, not parts, semantics, rigging, or graph-level structure.

Takeaway: O-Voxel is a strong asset-level representation, but it is still a finite-resolution voxel representation.

The appendix gives three explicit limitations. First, features smaller than the voxel size can alias. Two nearby parallel sheets inside one voxel are the clearest failure mode: the dual vertex minimizes error to both, so it may land between them rather than on either surface. The material feature in that voxel may also average incompatible surface materials.

Second, generated or reconstructed meshes can contain small holes. The paper says standard mesh post-processing such as hole filling can often rectify them, but it attributes the issue to the sparse decoder's difficulty in guaranteeing perfectly closed, manifold output. Third, O-Voxel currently encodes geometry and material only. It does not explicitly encode part segmentation, semantic labels, topology graphs, animation rigs, or editable object hierarchies.

10. Summary: the compact-native bet

Q: What should a reader carry forward from this paper?

Guide: compress the paper to one mechanism, one empirical result, and one caveat.

A: The mechanism is O-Voxel plus FDG: store mesh-derived geometry and PBR material directly on sparse active voxels, then compress that sparse 3D structure with SC-VAE. The empirical result is that Ours $1024^3$ leads Table 1 fidelity metrics and Ours leads Table 2 generation metrics, while keeping token counts far below high-resolution sparse-shape baselines. The caveat is that finite voxel resolution and sparse decoding still create aliasing and hole risks.

Takeaway: The paper's core value is a native 3D latent that is compact enough for large flow models and expressive enough for topology and material.

In one sentence: Native and Compact Structured Latents for 3D Generation argues that high-quality 3D generation needs a latent that starts from native mesh-and-material structure, not from a field or a view-fusion detour. The strongest evidence is reconstruction: at the same 9.6K token count as TRELLIS, Ours $1024^3$ is dramatically better across MD, CD, normal PSNR, and LPIPS. The generation table agrees, but automatic metric margins against Hunyuan3D 2.1 are small on CLIP-N and ULIP-2, so the user-study gap and the PBR material evidence carry much of the perceptual story. The implementation is also part of the result: without the sparse residual VAE and FlexGEMM backend, the native representation would be harder to train and scale.
