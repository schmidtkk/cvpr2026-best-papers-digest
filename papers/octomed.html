OctoMed: can a data recipe, not a new architecture, make a 7B medical reasoner competitive?

Paper2Html2026-06-25CVPR 2026 digest entry | Medical AI | UW-Madison / Microsoft ResearchSource: arXiv:2511.23269v1 PDF
Medical AIMultimodal ReasoningSupervised Fine-TuningData RecipeRejection SamplingChain-of-Thought8M ExamplesMathJax checked

The short version. OctoMed argues that multimodal medical reasoning is bottlenecked less by architecture novelty than by the supervised fine-tuning data recipe. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) means training a model on curated input-output examples; here those examples include chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning traces, which are intermediate text steps before the final answer. The recipe asks strong teachers to generate multiple traces, then uses rejection sampling to keep only traces whose final answer matches the ground truth. The final student is Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct, fully fine-tuned on 8,095,571 accepted instances and 6.8 billion response tokens. The strongest defensible headline is within the listed small open-source models: OctoMed reaches 67.83 text-only overall versus the next best small model GLM-4.1V at 61.84, 50.36 multimodal reasoning overall versus GLM-4.1V at 47.54, and 67.29 multimodal classification overall versus QoQ-Med at 56.24. It does not beat the largest systems overall: DeepSeek-R1 reaches 76.05 on text-only overall, and GPT-4o reaches 58.07 on multimodal reasoning overall. The cross-size surprise is narrower but real: OctoMed beats MedGemma-27B on the three overall rows, 67.83 versus 66.56, 50.36 versus 44.25, and 67.29 versus 50.97; it also beats GPT-4o on multimodal classification overall, 67.29 versus 53.96. Read the paper as evidence that scale plus carefully filtered reasoning traces can move a 7B medical VLM, not as proof of clinical readiness or universal superiority.

One-line contributionA medical multimodal reasoning dataset and SFT recipe built from teacher-generated reasoning traces, rejection sampling, multiple valid traces per question, and broad coverage across text, VQA, and diagnostic image classification.
Key mechanismFor each supervised problem, a teacher model generates reasoning steps and a final answer. A scoring function keeps only traces with the correct final answer, forming an accepted set $\mathcal{R}^+$. The student then learns from diverse correct paths rather than one canonical explanation.
Core evidenceAmong listed <10B open-source models, OctoMed is best on all three overall rows: 67.83 vs 61.84 text-only, 50.36 vs 47.54 multimodal reasoning, and 67.29 vs 56.24 multimodal classification. Against larger systems, it beats GPT-4o only on multimodal classification overall, 67.29 vs 53.96, while trailing GPT-4o on multimodal reasoning, 50.36 vs 58.07.
Reproduction boundaryThe PDF gives the model URL but no public training or data-construction code repository. It states final SFT settings, but teacher-call volume, GPU count, wall-clock time, and API cost are not stated in the paper.

1. Motivation: what is the real bottleneck?

Medical reasoning models face a harsher version of the usual vision-language problem. The model may see radiology, pathology, fundus photography, skin images, structured exam questions, and clinical notes. The target task may be diagnosis, visual question answering, or multi-step text reasoning. A narrow training mixture can look good in-domain and still fail when the modality, body region, or question style changes.

The authors frame the root question as a data problem: if SFT teaches the behavior that later post-training mostly refines, then the mixture of questions, modalities, and reasoning traces is not a detail. It is the main design surface. OctoMed therefore avoids proposing a new architecture, new loss, or reinforcement-learning objective. It asks which SFT examples should exist, how they should be filtered, and how many correct traces should be kept for each problem.

Q: Is this just "more data helps" with a medical label?

Guide: look for controlled choices, not only the final 8M scale.

A: No. The paper tests several ingredients before scaling them: source mixing, CoT versus direct prompting, question filtering, number of accepted traces per question, teacher choice, and model family. The 8M dataset matters, but the paper's useful claim is about which ingredients survive those tests.

Takeaway: OctoMed is best read as a recipe paper: build a high-coverage SFT mixture, keep only answer-correct traces, preserve multiple correct paths, and then scale.

2. Contributions: what the paper actually adds

The paper states three main findings and one dataset contribution. First, the authors train OctoMed and report state-of-the-art performance among listed small open-source models on the three overall rows: 67.83 versus 61.84 text-only, 50.36 versus 47.54 multimodal reasoning, and 67.29 versus 56.24 multimodal classification. Second, they show that multiple accepted reasoning traces per question improve peak performance more than simply repeating the same data for more epochs. Third, they observe task-aware reasoning length: after training on mixed trace lengths, the model tends to write longer traces on harder reasoning tasks and shorter traces on simpler tasks. The dataset contribution is the 8,095,571-instance medical multimodal reasoning SFT corpus summarized in Figure 2.

The more careful reader inference is narrower than the strongest abstract wording. OctoMed is not a universal winner over proprietary or much larger systems. Its text-only overall score is 67.83, below GPT-4o at 72.77 and DeepSeek-R1 at 76.05. Its multimodal reasoning overall score is 50.36, below GPT-4o at 58.07. The paper is strongest where it compares against similar-size open-source models and where the student beats MedGemma-27B or GPT-4o on selected categories.

How this page reads "state of the art"

"State of the art" is treated as category- and comparison-set-specific. In Table 2, OctoMed is best among the listed small open-source models on all three overall rows. It also beats MedGemma-27B on the three overall rows, 67.83 versus 66.56, 50.36 versus 44.25, and 67.29 versus 50.97. It does not beat every large/proprietary row, and this page does not state that it does.

3. Dataset: 8M accepted traces, but not one uniform benchmark

OctoMed's corpus is a patchwork of supervised medical problems converted into a reasoning-trace SFT dataset. The paper groups its sources into text-only reasoning, multimodal reasoning, and multimodal classification. Text-only sources include USMLE-style datasets such as MedQA, HeadQA, and MedMCQA. Multimodal reasoning uses medical VQA-style questions; because several reasoning benchmarks lack train splits, the authors use distilled traces from a subset of PMC-VQA as that question source. Multimodal classification covers diagnostic image tasks such as Aptos and MESSIDOR-2 fundus classification, BCSS pathology, and brain tumor MRI.

The preprocessing rules matter because medical benchmark leakage is easy. For text-only benchmarks, the authors run 16-gram deduplication between evaluation questions and all training questions. Q: Why not train only on the source that gives the largest gain?

A: Because each source overfits its own kind of task. Multimodal classification sources give +34.5% on classification but -23.7% on text-only and -13.6% on multimodal reasoning. Text-only sources help text-only reasoning but hurt classification. The text+multimodal-reasoning row is positive everywhere, but its classification gain is only +4.8%. The all-source row keeps every category positive and has the highest overall gain, +17.7%.

Takeaway: In this paper, coverage is a robustness tool. The mixed source row trades away some per-category maximum for the strongest overall recipe.

4. Pipeline: generate traces, filter by answer, then imitate

The distillation loop is intentionally simple. Let $\mathcal{D}=\{(x_i,y_i)\}_{i=1}^N$ be a supervised dataset of medical problems. The input $x_i$ is a clinical question or multimodal prompt, and $y_i$ is the ground-truth answer. For multiple-choice tasks, $y_i\in\mathcal{Y}$, where $\mathcal{Y}$ is the finite answer-option set.

A teacher model $\mathcal{T}$ generates intermediate reasoning steps $r_i=(r_i^{(1)},\ldots,r_i^{(T_i)})$ and a final answer $\hat{y}_i$. The scoring function checks whether that final answer is correct. Because nearly all training tasks are verifiable multiple-choice questions, the paper can use final-answer equality as the quality gate.

$$ S(x_i,r_i,y_i,\hat{y}_i)= \begin{cases} 1,&\text{if }\hat{y}_i=y_i,\\ 0,&\text{otherwise.} \end{cases} $$
Eq. 1: rejection-sampling score from the paper. The score is 1 only when the teacher's final answer matches the ground truth.
$$ \mathcal{R}^+=\left\{(x_i,y_i,r_i)\mid S(x_i,r_i,y_i,\hat{y}_i)=1\right\}. $$
Eq. 2: accepted reasoning traces. The student model $\sigma$ is fine-tuned on $\mathcal{R}^+$.

What the gate does, and what it does not do

The gate validates the final answer, not the entire chain of reasoning. That is practical for multiple-choice medical data, because the answer can be checked cheaply. It also leaves a risk: a trace may end with the right letter while containing a flawed intermediate claim. The paper's rejection sampling is therefore a strong answer filter, not a full medical fact verifier.

Q: Why keep several correct traces for the same question?

Guide: a repeated epoch shows the same path again; a new accepted trace shows another path to the same answer.

A: Multiple traces make the supervision less brittle. The answer stays fixed, but the intermediate path changes. The student cannot win by memorizing one explanation template. It must learn the common structure that makes several explanations land on the same correct answer.

Takeaway: Rejection sampling supplies quality; multiple accepted traces supply diversity.

5. Method: the data decisions that carry the result

The method section has no new architecture to diagram. The mechanism is a set of data decisions that change what the student sees during SFT. The paper tests those decisions one by one.

5.1 CoT is useful for reasoning, but not universally useful

Chain-of-thought prompting asks the model to produce an internal reasoning trace before answering. Direct prompting asks for the answer immediately. Table 1 shows why the final recipe uses CoT broadly but why the choice is not cost-free.

5.2 More accepted traces beat more repetition

The MedQA experiment isolates the trace-count question. The teacher generates 16 responses per question. The authors then keep at most 1, 4, or 16 valid traces per question and train each setting for 3 epochs, producing 9 checkpoints.

Q: Why is this stronger than simply saying "train longer"?

A: The one-trace model trained three epochs sees the same reasoning path repeatedly and reaches 75.16. The four-trace model trained one epoch sees different correct paths and reaches 76.50. More importantly, the 16-trace model reaches 85.01 after three epochs. Repetition helps optimization; trace diversity changes the supervision distribution.

Takeaway: The paper's most convincing mechanism evidence is that accepted-trace diversity changes the peak, not only the speed.

6. Training and evaluation setup: what is disclosed

The paper gives two sets of training settings. The data-recipe experiments use full fine-tuning with batch size 128, learning rate 5e-5, and a cosine scheduler with 0.01 linear warmup ratio. The final OctoMed model is fully fine-tuned on the 8M-trace dataset for 3 epochs using llamafactory, learning rate 5e-5, effective batch size 512, and a cosine scheduler with linear warmup ratio 0.1. The student model is Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct.

8. Ablations: what survives controlled changes?

8.1 Filtering helps early efficiency, not the final peak

The authors test three pre-teacher question filters on PMC-VQA. Student proportion filtering drops questions where the student gets fewer than 2 or more than 14 of 16 samples correct. Teacher proportion filtering applies the same idea with the teacher. LLM-judge filtering asks GPT-4.1-mini to rate question-answer difficulty from 1 to 10 and keeps scores 3 through 6.

Q: Why keep unfiltered questions if filtering improves early training?

A: Because the paper is optimizing final coverage under a large SFT budget, not only early sample efficiency. Rejection sampling already removes teacher traces that end with wrong answers. Once the dataset is large enough, the extra coverage from unfiltered questions is more valuable than the early-speed gain from filtering.

Takeaway: In this recipe, pre-filtering is an efficiency tool; rejection sampling is the main quality gate.

8.2 Task-aware length appears without explicit length labels

The paper's task-aware length result comes from mixed trace lengths in the training data. The authors compare OctoMed against QoQ-Med-VL-7B, a strong baseline trained from the same base model. Figure 8 reports token usage and performance across in-domain and out-of-distribution tasks.

Q: What is the most likely overstatement?

A: "A 7B model beats GPT-4o for medical reasoning." The table says something more precise: OctoMed beats GPT-4o on multimodal classification overall, 67.29 versus 53.96, but trails GPT-4o on multimodal reasoning overall, 50.36 versus 58.07, and text-only overall, 67.83 versus 72.77.

Takeaway: The useful lesson is not that small models now dominate large ones. It is that high-quality trace data can move a 7B model into the same competitive region for several medical benchmark families.

10. Summary: the useful mental model

In one sentence: OctoMed turns medical multimodal reasoning into a data-recipe problem: sample many teacher traces, keep answer-correct ones, preserve multiple correct paths, mix source categories broadly, and let SFT absorb that structure.

Three takeaways

  1. The data mix is the method. Same-source training gives sharp in-category gains, but the all-source mix gives the strongest overall improvement in Figure 3, +17.7%.
  2. Trace diversity matters. On MedQA, 16 accepted traces per question reach 85.01 after 3 epochs, compared with 75.16 for one trace.
  3. Reasoning length becomes task-sensitive. OctoMed writes long traces for hard reasoning tasks and short traces for simpler perception tasks, but that behavior should be treated as a correlation with task difficulty, not a proof that longer is always better.

The practical reading path is: start with Figure 2 for the dataset scale, read Eq. 1 and Eq. 2 for the filtering gate, use Table 1 and Figure 5 to understand why the recipe keeps CoT and multiple accepted traces, then read Table 2 with the limitation that OctoMed is strongest among small open-source models and selected larger-model comparisons.
