OraPO: can failed GRPO samples become the supervision signal?

Paper2Html2026-06-25CVPR 2026 digest entry | Medical AI | Oracle Health & AI / University of QueenslandSource: arXiv:2509.18600v2 PDF
Medical AIRadiology Report GenerationGRPODirect Preference OptimizationFactScore RewardData-efficient RLMathJax checked

The short version. Radiology report generation (RRG) asks a model to turn chest X-rays into clinically useful free text, so the output must cover the findings, not merely sound like a report. OraPO starts from a budget constraint: the paper trains a 3B Qwen2.5-VL vision-language model (VLM) with single-stage reinforcement learning (RL) on 1,000 studies per dataset, instead of the common multi-stage recipe over hundreds of thousands or millions of image-report pairs. The failure it targets is specific: early Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) groups often receive all-zero rewards, so the group-normalized advantage carries almost no task signal. OraPO treats those failed rollouts as ready-made rejected answers, pairs them with the ground-truth report, and applies a Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) update exactly where GRPO is weak. The reward is FactS, a FactScore-style label reward that extracts atomic clinical facts and scores their entailment against the 14 CheXpert labels; macro Precision, Recall, and F1 are unweighted averages across those labels. On CheXpert Plus, OraPO reports macro F1 0.341 using 1K training studies, narrowly above MambaXray-L's 0.335 trained on 1.27M, but the same comparison also shows the trade-off: recall is 0.832 versus 0.319 and precision is lower, 0.237 versus 0.377. On MIMIC-CXR, OraPO reports macro F1 0.357 versus MambaXray-L's 0.340, again with much higher recall, 0.891 versus 0.321, and lower precision, 0.242 versus 0.371. On the CheXpert human-gold set, OraPO's F1 0.288 beats GPT-4.1 at 0.253 and MambaXray-L at 0.280, but it is below GPT-5 Thinking at 0.301 and has the lowest precision among the listed systems.

One-line contributionOraPO couples GRPO with DPO so that zero- or low-reward radiology report rollouts become rejected samples instead of wasted samples.
Key mechanismA zero-reward-rate (ZRR) signal raises the DPO mixing weight on prompts where GRPO has little reward variation, then lets GRPO dominate again as the model begins to receive non-zero rewards.
Core evidenceOn CheXpert Plus macro clinical effectiveness, OraPO reaches F1 0.341 with 1K training studies, just above MambaXray-L's 0.335 with 1.27M studies; the same row must be read with recall 0.832 versus 0.319 and precision 0.237 versus 0.377, so this is a recall-oriented win, not a clean precision win.
Reproduction boundaryThe PDF discloses the base model, 4 A10 GPUs, batch size, group size, learning rate, ZRR parameters, and reward formula. It does not state wall-clock training time, API cost for fact extraction or entailment, full prompts, or a code repository.

1. Motivation: why does GRPO stall in radiology reports?

GRPO is attractive because it removes the trainable value critic used in Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO). For each image and prompt, it samples several reports, scores them, normalizes those scores within the group, and updates the policy from relative advantage. That design is efficient when at least one sample in the group earns a useful reward.

Radiology report generation violates that assumption early in training. A general VLM has limited chest-X-ray reporting knowledge, so it may sample a whole group of reports that miss the relevant findings. The paper reports that in the first 50 optimization steps on CheXpert Plus, roughly 30% of groups have all rewards equal to zero. A group with no reward variation produces almost no relative advantage. The sampler has spent compute, but the update has little task signal.

Q: Why does an all-zero group hurt GRPO more than ordinary low performance?

Guide: GRPO does not only ask whether a sample is good. It asks whether one sample is better than the other samples for the same prompt.

A: If all samples are equally bad, the group contains no preference ordering. The group mean is zero and every reward equals the mean, so the advantage term is near zero for every sampled report. The clipped policy-ratio part of GRPO then has almost nothing to optimize. The remaining pressure is mostly the Kullback-Leibler (KL) term that keeps the policy close to the frozen reference.

Takeaway: The bad samples are not useless in principle; they are useless only to a relative-reward objective that cannot rank them.

OraPO's central move is to change the interpretation of those samples. A failed report is uninformative to GRPO, but it is informative to DPO if paired against the ground-truth report. In DPO language, the ground truth is the chosen response and the failed rollout is the rejected response. The same model output therefore changes from "zero advantage" to "a clear negative example."

2. Contributions: what the paper actually adds

The authors state three contributions. First, they propose OraPO, an oracle-educated GRPO algorithm that integrates direct preference learning with GRPO-based RL. Second, they design FactS, a FactScore-based reward that converts a generated report into atomic clinical facts and checks those facts against ground-truth labels. Third, they report the best macro clinical-effectiveness F1 in their CheXpert Plus table, 0.341 versus MambaXray-L's 0.335, and in their MIMIC-CXR macro table, 0.357 versus MambaXray-L's 0.340; both comparisons use far fewer training samples and both have lower precision than the MambaXray-L row.

The important narrowing is in the phrase "clinical-effectiveness F1." The main tables evaluate 14 CheXpert pathology labels, not text overlap. OraPO does not try to maximize ROUGE-style surface similarity, and the precision column shows the cost of its recall-oriented reward. The paper is strongest as an argument about sample efficiency and missed-finding reduction, not as a blanket claim that its reports are more precise or more human-like in every respect.

How to read the headline

"SOTA" in this page means "best in the paper's reported macro clinical-effectiveness F1 table under the stated benchmark setup." It does not mean best precision, best report wording, or clinical deployment readiness. Whenever the page says OraPO beats a baseline, the competing value and the setup appear in the same sentence.

3. Data: small training splits, label-level evaluation

OraPO is evaluated on two chest-X-ray report datasets. CheXpert Plus links chest-X-ray DICOMs to de-identified radiology reports and labels 14 target pathologies. The public release contains about 223K image-report pairs, about 187.7K studies, about 64.7K patients, and about 36 million report tokens. MIMIC-CXR contains 377,110 images from 227,943 imaging studies covering 65,379 patients, with one or more views and a free-text report per study.

The training split is deliberately tiny. For data-efficient fine-tuning, the authors sample 1,000 studies per dataset, create a balanced ground-truth label set with the CheXpert labeller, a label extractor used in the paper, and exclude reports with missing sections. On CheXpert Plus, 1,000 studies are about 0.45% of the standard 223K split and about 0.079% of the 1.27M examples used by MambaXray.

The metric is macro clinical effectiveness. For each of the 14 conditions, the evaluator computes Precision, Recall, and F1 on extracted labels, then averages each metric across labels without frequency weighting. That choice gives rare findings the same weight as common findings. It also explains why OraPO's main benefit appears as high recall across labels.

Q: Does "1K studies" mean the method no longer needs data?

A: No. It changes what each study is used for. In supervised fine-tuning (SFT), a study mostly provides one target report to imitate. In OraPO, the same study provides a ground-truth report for preference pairs and a label set for fact-level rewards across multiple generated reports. The supervision becomes denser, but it still depends on reliable reports and labels.

Takeaway: The data claim is not "data is unnecessary"; it is "failed samples can be recycled so each labeled study does more work."

4. Pipeline: two update paths for the same rollout group

OraPO starts from Qwen2.5-VL-3B-Instruct. For each chest X-ray and report-generation prompt, the policy samples a group of reports. FactS scores each sampled report by extracting atomic clinical facts and checking which CheXpert labels those facts entail. If rewards vary within the group, GRPO can compute useful relative advantages. If many rewards are zero, OraPO increases the DPO branch, where the ground-truth report is preferred over the failed generated reports.

5. Method: from zero advantage to preference gradient

5.1 GRPO and the all-zero failure

GRPO samples a group of reports for the same image-prompt pair and normalizes their rewards inside that group. The group mean and scale are:

$$\bar r=\frac{1}{K}\sum_{j=1}^{K}r_j,\qquad \sigma=\sqrt{\frac{1}{K}\sum_{j=1}^{K}(r_j-\bar r)^2+\varepsilon}$$
Eq. 1: within-group reward mean and scale.

The advantage for sample $j$ is then:

$$A_j=\frac{r_j-\bar r}{\sigma}$$
Eq. 2: group-normalized advantage.

The policy ratio compares the current policy with the behavior policy used to sample the group:

$$\rho_j=\exp\!\left(\log \pi_{\theta}(\hat y_j\mid x,p)-\log \pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}(\hat y_j\mid x,p)\right)$$
Eq. 3: PPO-style policy ratio.

The GRPO objective uses the clipped ratio and a KL regularizer to the frozen reference policy:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GRPO}}=-\mathbb{E}\!\left[\min\!\left(\rho_jA_j,\operatorname{clip}(\rho_j,1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon)A_j\right)\right]+\lambda_{\mathrm{KL}}\mathbb{E}\!\left[\mathrm{KL}\!\left(\pi_{\theta}(\cdot\mid x,p)\,\middle\|\,\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(\cdot\mid x,p)\right)\right]$$
Eq. 4: GRPO loss with clipped advantage term and KL regularization.

Why Eq. 1-4 explain the failure

If every reward in a group is zero, then $\bar r=0$ and every numerator $r_j-\bar r$ is zero. Eq. 2 makes every $A_j$ near zero. Eq. 4 then loses the task-reward part of the update and mostly keeps the policy near $\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}$. The sampled reports are bad, but GRPO has no within-group contrast to learn from.

5.2 DPO turns the same bad samples into rejected answers

DPO needs a chosen response and a rejected response. OraPO uses the ground-truth report as chosen and the sampled report as rejected. The DPO log-probability margins are:

$$\Delta^+=\log \pi_{\theta}(y^+\mid x,p)-\log \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y^+\mid x,p),\qquad \Delta^-=\log \pi_{\theta}(y^-\mid x,p)-\log \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y^-\mid x,p)$$
Eq. 5-6: policy-minus-reference margins for chosen and rejected reports.
$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{DPO}}=-\mathbb{E}\!\left[\log \sigma_{\mathrm{logistic}}\!\left(\tau(\Delta^+-\Delta^-)\right)\right]$$
Eq. 7: DPO loss; $\tau$ is the temperature.

The key is the change in viewpoint. A generated report with zero FactS reward gives no advantage under GRPO, but it is a useful negative under DPO. The paper calls the positive response an oracle because it is the ground-truth report, not because an extra human is queried during training.

5.3 ZRR decides how much DPO to inject

OraPO measures how often a group contains zero-reward completions. The printed formula is a fraction of zero-reward completions:

$$z_i=\frac{1}{K}\sum_{j=1}^{K}\mathbf{1}[r_{i,j}=0]$$
Eq. 8: zero-reward rate for prompt $i$.

The endpoints are the important cases: $z_i=1$ means every sampled report received zero reward, while $z_i=0$ means none did. Intermediate values mean mixed groups. OraPO smooths this signal with an exponential moving average:

$$\tilde z_i^{(t)}=\alpha \tilde z_i^{(t-1)}+(1-\alpha)z_i^{(t)},\qquad \alpha\in[0,1)$$
Eq. 9: EMA-smoothed ZRR.

The smoothed ZRR is mapped to a bounded mixing weight:

$$w_i^{(t)}=\operatorname{clip}\!\left(w_{\min}+(w_{\max}-w_{\min})\left[\tilde z_i^{(t)}\right]^{\gamma},\,w_{\min},\,w_{\max}\right)$$
Eq. 10: ZRR-to-DPO weight map.

The final OraPO objective mixes GRPO and DPO per prompt:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{OraPO}}=\frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B}\left[(1-w_i^{(t)})\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GRPO}}(x_i,p_i)+w_i^{(t)}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{DPO}}(x_i,p_i)\right]$$
Eq. 11: batch objective with adaptive DPO mixing.

The preference pair is constructed directly from the sampled group:

$$y_i^+=y_i^\star,\qquad y_i^-\in\{\hat y_{i,j}\}_{j=1}^{K}\sim \pi_{\theta}(\cdot\mid x_i,p_i)$$
Eq. 12: ground truth is chosen; generated reports are rejected.

Q: Why not use DPO all the time?

A: Because DPO is corrective, not exploratory. When rewards are all zero, DPO gives the missing direction. Once the model starts producing some rewarded reports, GRPO can compare alternatives and improve from exploration. The ZRR weight is the switch between those two regimes.

Takeaway: OraPO is not "GRPO plus a fixed DPO loss." It is a feedback controller: more DPO when GRPO stalls, less DPO when reward variation returns.

5.4 FactS defines what "good report" means

FactS treats a generated report as its own rationale. First, GPT-4.1 is prompted to extract atomic clinical facts:

$$F(\hat y_i)=\{s_{i,k}\}_{k=1}^{K_i}$$
Eq. 13: atomic facts extracted from the generated report.

Second, an LLM-based entailment check maps those facts to the label set:

$$\hat z_{i,\ell}=\mathbf{1}\!\left[\exists s\in F(\hat y_i)\ \mathrm{s.t.}\ \operatorname{entails}(s,\ell)=\mathrm{true}\right],\qquad \ell\in L$$
Eq. 14: label prediction by fact entailment.

Contradictions are counted as false positives for the relevant label. Finally, the reward is an $F_\beta$ score computed from per-instance precision $P_i$ and recall $R_i$:

$$r(x_i,\hat y_i)=F_{\beta,i}=\frac{(1+\beta^2)P_iR_i}{\beta^2P_i+R_i+\xi}$$
Eq. 15: FactS reward, with $\beta\gt1$ placing more weight on recall.

The clinical stance is now explicit. By setting $\beta\gt1$, the reward penalizes missed positive findings more strongly than extra false positives. That design aligns with the paper's recall-oriented objective, but it also explains why precision is consistently lower than several baselines.

6. Training and compute: what is disclosed

The paper trains a 3B Qwen2.5-VL policy in an RL-only setting with no SFT/alignment stage. It uses Hugging Face TRL's GRPOTrainer with transformers and accelerate, PyTorch 2.7.1, CUDA 12.1, and four NVIDIA A10 GPUs with 24 GB memory each. The PDF states the hardware and hyperparameters, but it does not state wall-clock training time or total GPU-hours, so no compute bill is estimated here.

Q: Why does the SFT row collapse while the DPO row improves recall?

A: The paper's explanation is that SFT imitates the ground-truth prose but does not explicitly push probability away from reports that omit true positives. In this small-data setting, that can overfit and become conservative. DPO is contrastive: it lowers the probability of sampled failures while raising the ground-truth report. Because many sampled failures are missing-finding reports, that contrast directly improves recall.

Takeaway: The ablation supports the mechanism only if read in order: FactS supplies dense clinical reward, and OraPO recycles the failed rollouts that FactS exposes.

Scope of validationExperiments are chest-X-ray RRG on CheXpert Plus and MIMIC-CXR.The paper does not show the method on CT, MRI, non-radiology medical text, or non-medical sparse-reward tasks.
Scaling unknownThe paper uses a 3B policy and 1K studies per dataset.Scaling beyond 3B, beyond 1K, and across larger RL budgets is listed as future work.
Compute disclosure incompleteThe PDF states 4 A10 GPUs and main hyperparameters.Wall-clock time, total GPU-hours, and reward-model call cost are not stated in the paper.
Clinical deployment not establishedThe paper reports benchmark tables, a 200-study human-gold validation set, and qualitative examples.These support research progress in report generation, not autonomous clinical reporting.

Q: What is the most likely overstatement?

A: "OraPO is simply better at radiology reporting." The paper supports a narrower claim: under macro label-level clinical-effectiveness evaluation, OraPO uses very little training data to get high recall and strong F1. It does not remove the precision trade-off or prove fine-grained clinical correctness.

Takeaway: The method is best understood as a sample-efficient, recall-oriented RL recipe for label-faithful chest-X-ray reporting.

10. Summary: the useful mental model

In one sentence: OraPO notices that the rollout group GRPO cannot rank is exactly the rollout group DPO can use as rejected evidence, and it uses FactS plus ZRR to decide when to make that switch.

Three takeaways

  1. The same failed sample has two meanings. Under GRPO it has zero relative advantage; under DPO it is a rejected report paired against ground truth.
  2. FactS makes reward dense enough for RRG. It extracts atomic facts, maps them to the 14 CheXpert labels, and uses $F_\beta$ so missed findings matter more than surface wording.
  3. The evidence is strong but scoped. OraPO wins macro F1 on CheXpert Plus and MIMIC-CXR with 1K studies, but it does so through high recall and lower precision.

A good reading path through the paper is: start with Eq. 1-4 to see why all-zero groups waste GRPO updates; then read Eq. 8-12 to see how ZRR controls the DPO branch; then read Table 4 before the main leaderboards, because it shows what FactS and DPO each add.
