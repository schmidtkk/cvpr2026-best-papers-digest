SA-FARI: the dataset bottleneck behind camera-trap multi-animal tracking

Paper2Html2026-06-26CVPR 2026 Top Paper · Conservation X Labs and Meta, with partner institutionsSource: arXiv:2511.15622v2 PDF
DatasetCamera TrapsMulti-Animal TrackingHuman-Verified MaskletsSAM 3MathJax checked

The short version. Multi-animal tracking, or MAT, is the task of localizing each animal through time, so later systems can estimate abundance, recognize species or individuals, and analyze behavior. A camera trap is a motion-triggered field camera that records wildlife without a human operator, often at night with infrared illumination. SA-FARI is a dataset paper about the missing training substrate for that task. Its core annotation unit is a masklet: one animal's spatio-temporal mask tube across video frames, with a consistent identity over time. The paper evaluates trackers with HOTA, Higher Order Tracking Accuracy, a metric that balances detection accuracy and association accuracy, so a model must both find animals and keep their identities connected. The claim is not just that SA-FARI is bigger. The claim is that it combines scale, species breadth, site and time diversity, and human-verified spatio-temporal segmentation in one camera-trap MAT dataset. The evidence is strongest when the paper uses SA-FARI to train or fine-tune strong models, then shows large gains and diagnostic failures on small, moving, occluded, multi-animal, and night videos.

Dataset scale11,609 camera-trap videos from 741 independent sampling locations, 4 continents, and roughly 10 years of collection from 2014 to 2024.
Annotation unit16,224 human-verified masklet identities and 942,702 frame-level box and mask annotations across 2,747 annotated minutes, or 45.78 hours.
Species scope99 species categories: 67 mammal categories, 27 bird categories, 4 reptile categories, and 1 amphibian category.
Main resultFine-tuning SAM 3 on SA-FARI improves cgF1 from 14.0 to 46.9, a 3.35x gain; total pHOTA rises from 48.5 to 68.1.

1. Motivation: the dataset gap is the method gap

Biodiversity monitoring needs more than image classification. Conservation teams often need to know which species appeared, how many individuals were present, how they moved, and whether the same individual reappeared later. Those questions require localization through space and time, not just a label on a clip.

That is where MAT becomes foundational. MAT is a specific case of multi-object tracking, or MOT, where the objects are animals. The tracker must detect or segment animals in each frame and preserve identity links across frames. If the links fail, downstream tasks such as individual re-identification and behavior recognition inherit the mistake.

The paper argues that wild-animal MAT has lagged because existing datasets force a trade-off. Some datasets are small, with less than one hour of annotated footage for dedicated MAT benchmarks. Some cover only a few species, often no more than five in larger wild-animal resources. Some are geographically narrow, such as one protected area over a short collection window. Some include segmentation masks, but those masks are generated automatically without manual correction, so quality is uncertain.

SA-FARI is designed to remove that trade-off. It is a camera-trap dataset, so it matches the sensors conservation teams already deploy. It spans many sites, years, ecoregions, and species. Most importantly, it uses human-verified masklets rather than unverified automatic masks.

Q: Why does the paper care so much about segmentation rather than just boxes?

A: Boxes say where an animal roughly is. Masks say which pixels belong to the animal. For wildlife, that extra precision matters because coat patterns, body shape, pose, and partial occlusion can be the signal for species, individual identity, or behavior. A masklet also keeps that precise animal shape tied to one identity over time.

Takeaway: The dataset is not only a tracking benchmark. It is a source of precise spatio-temporal animal representations.

2. Contribution: four things at once

The paper's contribution is a dataset, not a new tracking architecture. SA-FARI consolidates camera-trap video from partner organizations into an open-source benchmark for wild-animal MAT. The dataset contains 11,609 videos, 99 species categories, 741 independent sampling sites, 16,224 masklets, and 942,702 box and mask annotations.

The authors state that SA-FARI has 5x greater total annotated duration and 2x broader species diversity than any existing MAT dataset. The species claim is well supported by the comparison table: 99 categories is about 1.94x the 51 fauna categories reported for TAO/BURST. The duration claim needs a marker. In the broad comparison table below, the largest listed duration outside SA-FARI is MammAlps at 14.50 hours, so the raw table ratio is 45.78 / 14.50 = 3.16x. The paper's 5x statement appears in its contribution prose; the table-backed comparison is 3.16x against the broadest listed row, and larger if the comparison excludes related behavior or re-identification datasets that were not built primarily as MAT benchmarks.

The higher-confidence claim is narrower and more important: SA-FARI is the first large-scale wild-animal MAT dataset in the paper's comparison that combines camera-trap scale, many species, many independent sites, and manually verified spatio-temporal segmentation masks.

3. Dataset: what SA-FARI contains

The data comes from four broad ecoregions: Central Africa, South America, Mesoamerica, and Southern Europe. The paper names seven partner sources, including Osa Conservation, Los Amigos Biological Station, IREC, IMIB, the Pan African Programme: The Cultured Chimpanzee, and Conservation X Labs. Camera traps came from several commercial brands and models. Most were placed 0.3 m to 2 m from the ground at wildlife crossings, gathering sites, feeding sites, resting sites, or potential viewing sites. A subset of 2,790 Costa Rica videos came from arboreal camera traps placed 8 m to 24 m above the ground.

The videos cover resolutions from 320x194 to 2688x1234, frame rates from 10 to 60 FPS, and durations from 0.5 s to 90 s, with most clips lasting 15 s. All videos contain at least one animal. All videos also contain audio, but audio is not used in this work.

Species labels use common names such as fox, then the dataset also provides Latin names and taxonomic hierarchy down to at least family and usually species level where appropriate. Each video's common-name label was confirmed by 2 to 4 independent annotators. Videos containing multiple species were disambiguated during masklet annotation by assigning common names to the corresponding tracklets.

4. Pipeline: a human-AI-in-the-loop annotation engine

The spatio-temporal segmentation annotations are created as part of the SAM 3 data engine. SAM means Segment Anything Model. SAM 2 is the image-and-video version used by annotators as an online correction tool. SAM 3 extends that family to open-vocabulary segmentation and tracking, so a text concept such as a species name can prompt localization through a video.

A human-AI-in-the-loop pipeline means model predictions are not released as-is. The model proposes annotations, humans inspect and correct them, and a final check verifies completeness. In SA-FARI the process has three stages.

  1. Automatic pseudo-annotation. SAM 3 pseudo-annotates every video-species category pair at 6 FPS, generating initial masks. The system deduplicates masklets using Intersection over Union, or IoU, the overlap ratio between two masks.
  2. Human correction with SAM 2 in the loop. A first human annotator checks whether the animal is visible and segmentable. A second annotator removes wrong masklets and uses online SAM 2 to improve or add masklets.
  3. Exhaustiveness check. A final review confirms that all possible masklets have been included.

Bounding boxes are not manually drawn. They are derived from the segmentation masks by computing each mask's spatial limits. That matters because the dataset's primary supervision is pixel-level segmentation, while the box labels come for free from those masks.

The paper does not state total human annotator-hours or per-masklet verification cost. It does state the video duration that receives dense annotation: 2,747 minutes, or 45.78 hours.

5. Method details: splitting, negatives, and metrics

The train/test split is site-disjoint. The authors cap the test set at 1,000 videos, then greedily select camera traps to maximize species diversity while keeping all videos from the same camera trap in the same split. The result is 833 test videos from 91 independent sites and 10,776 train videos from 650 independent sites.

SA-FARI also adds negative species labels for training and evaluation. A negative species is a species that does not appear in a given video. The paper groups species into 29 taxonomic groups (a separate count from the 29 head categories above, which happen to match), then adds one hard negative from the same group and one easy negative from a different group for each video-species pair. This is possible because the positive species labels are exhaustive.

The paper uses promptable tracking metrics because models receive a species name or generic animal prompt. IDF1 measures how accurately a tracker keeps identities consistent across detections. HOTA, Higher Order Tracking Accuracy, combines detection and association. In the simplified form requested here, with DetA as detection accuracy and AssA as association accuracy:

$$\mathrm{HOTA}=\sqrt{\mathrm{DetA}\times\mathrm{AssA}}$$
Higher is better: a model must detect animals and preserve identity associations.

pHOTA, phrase-based HOTA, adapts HOTA to open-vocabulary tracking by treating each video-phrase pair as a sample. TETA, Track Every Thing Accuracy, extends HOTA with localization, association, and classification terms. cgF1, classification-gated F1, rewards both localization quality and calibrated image-level concept prediction. MOTA, Multiple Object Tracking Accuracy, is the older MOT aggregate that penalizes misses, false positives, and identity switches; the paper does not report MOTA in the transcribed main tables. For all of these metrics as used here, higher is better.

Q: Why use HOTA-style metrics instead of only detection F1?

A: Detection F1 can reward a model for finding animals in isolated frames. MAT also needs the model to keep the same animal identity connected over time. HOTA-style metrics make that second requirement visible through association accuracy.

Takeaway: SA-FARI is useful only if evaluation punishes identity breaks, not just missed boxes or masks.

6. Training setup: what is actually tested

The benchmark has two main settings. In the species-specific prompt setting, a vision-language model receives the target species category as text and must localize and track every instance of that species through the video. The paper evaluates GLEE, LLMDet paired with a SAM 3 tracker, and SAM 3.

The SAM 3 rows are the cleanest training-value evidence because they compare the same model family under different data exposure. The three variants are base SAM 3, SAM 3 with SA-FARI included proportionally during training, and SAM 3 explicitly fine-tuned on SA-FARI.

The second setting is species-agnostic prompt evaluation. Here the species names are replaced with the generic prompt "animal." The paper compares SAM 3 trained on SA-FARI against MegaDetector paired with ByteTrack, OCSort, or BoostSort++. The SAM 3 models trained on SA-FARI were not exposed to the "animal" query during training; the prompt is used only at inference time.

7. Experiments: does SA-FARI help train trackers?

The strongest result is that domain-specific SA-FARI data helps a state-of-the-art model. In the species-specific setting, fine-tuned SAM 3 improves from 14.0 to 46.9 cgF1, from 48.5 to 68.1 total pHOTA, and from 39.6 to 58.7 TETA. The "three-fold" statement in the conclusion applies cleanly to cgF1: 46.9 / 14.0 = 3.35x. Total pHOTA improves by 19.6 points, while its detection component improves by 27.0 points.

8. Diagnostic value: what the test subsets reveal

SA-FARI is not only a training set. The paper also partitions the test set to expose failure modes. A masklet is challenging if the animal is moving, measured by average inter-frame IoU \(\lt 0.7\), or if it has \(\ge 2\) occlusions. Night videos are all videos recorded at night. Multi-masklet videos contain two or more animals, regardless of species. Large-masklet videos are above the 75th percentile of average masklet size, and small-masklet videos are below the 25th percentile.

The diagnostic pattern is useful because the failures differ. Small masklets are much harder than large masklets: total pHOTA drops from 81.4 to 52.2, and detection pHOTA drops from 72.5 to 38.5. The challenging subset has similar detection difficulty to the full set, with pHOTA Det 51.3 versus 55.4, but much worse association, with pHOTA Ass 75.3 versus 84.6. That matches the paper's interpretation: small targets hurt detection, while motion and occlusion hurt identity association.

Multiple-animal videos are close to the full set on total pHOTA, 67.8 versus 68.1, partly because those samples tend to contain larger masks. Night videos are also close to the full set, though slightly lower on cgF1 and pHOTA. The point is not that night is easy; it is that SA-FARI lets the reader separate night effects from size, occlusion, and association errors.

9. Limitations and caveats

Boundaries stated or implied by the paper. Each caveat is listed once.
BoundaryWhat the paper saysHow to read it
Camera-trap domainThe dataset is built from camera-trap video.It is directly relevant to conservation camera-trap workflows, not a universal video-tracking benchmark for all animal footage.
Fixed taxonomySA-FARI has 99 species categories and provides common names plus taxonomic hierarchy.The benchmark is broad but still bounded by the species and regions in the collected data.
Geographic biasThe conclusion says future integration should prioritize additional ecoregions.The dataset is diverse, but not globally complete.
Annotation budgetThe paper describes multi-stage human verification but does not state annotator-hours.Quality is higher than automatic masks alone, but the human cost is not quantified in the paper.
Audio unusedAll videos contain audio, but it is not used in this work.Audio is a future multimodal opportunity, not part of the reported benchmark.
Model evidenceThe main model evidence is SAM 3 plus selected open-vocabulary and tracking baselines.The gains show value for strong promptable models, but they do not prove all future trackers will gain equally.

The most important caveat is about the comparative duration claim. The paper's prose says 5x; the broad table gives 3.16x against MammAlps. This does not weaken the dataset's main contribution, but it does change how carefully that headline should be repeated.

10. Summary: why SA-FARI matters

SA-FARI is a data paper with a simple thesis: wild-animal MAT has been waiting for a dataset that is large, diverse, camera-trap native, and verified at masklet level. The paper backs that thesis with 11,609 videos, 741 independent sites, 99 species categories, 16,224 masklets, and 942,702 masks and boxes.

The training results support the dataset's practical value. Fine-tuning SAM 3 on SA-FARI gives a 3.35x cgF1 gain over base SAM 3 and raises total pHOTA by 19.6 points. The diagnostic split results support a second value: SA-FARI can tell researchers whether a method fails because animals are small, because identity association breaks under motion and occlusion, or because the scene has multiple animals or night conditions.

The bottom line is calibrated. SA-FARI is not a finished solution to wildlife understanding. It is a large, human-verified camera-trap benchmark that makes the core failure modes measurable.
