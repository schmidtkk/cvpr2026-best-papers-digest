SPECTRE: how two-level attention makes full-volume CT transformers trainable

Paper2Html2026-06-11CVPR 2026 · Medical AI · Eindhoven University of TechnologySource: arXiv:2511.17209v2 PDF
Medical AIVolumetric CT3D Vision TransformerDINOv3 + SigLIP3D RoPEOpen DataMathJax checked

The short version. A computed tomography (CT) scan is a 3D volume, so a naive Vision Transformer (ViT) faces two scaling shocks at once. The number of 3D patches grows with the cube of resolution, because the patches tile a 3D grid, and self-attention then grows with the square of the patch count. SPECTRE answers with a two-level transformer. A local transformer, \(\mathrm{ViT}_{\ell}\), spends 24 layers inside high-resolution windows. A global transformer, \(\mathrm{ViT}_{g}\), spends only 4 layers on one descriptor per window. That split keeps fine anatomical detail local while letting the scan-level model see the whole volume. The second half of the paper is about supervision: first learn geometry from CT volumes with DINO-style self-supervision, then align full scans to radiology reports with SigLIP, a sigmoid contrastive loss that is better matched to noisy many-condition clinical reports than softmax CLIP. The evidence is broad but not uniform: SPECTRE is best on 4 of 6 frozen k-nearest-neighbor (kNN) biomarker benchmarks, is competitive but not the best convolutional segmenter, and clearly improves CT-RATE zero-shot retrieval over CT-CLIP.

One-line contributionA fully transformer-based CT foundation model that combines geometry-aware 3D tokenization, local-plus-global volumetric attention, DINOv3-style self-supervision, and SigLIP report alignment.
Key mechanismEach scan is split into \(G\) windows. \(\mathrm{ViT}_{\ell}\) extracts high-resolution features inside each window, then each window is compressed to \([cls]\) plus mean patch features. \(\mathrm{ViT}_{g}\) attends over the short \(G+1\) scan sequence.
Core evidenceFrozen kNN biomarker classification is best on 4/6 datasets; CT-RATE Recall@5 rises from 2.9 for CT-CLIP to 17.5; SEoMT segmentation beats the transformer baselines on Table 1's three datasets — though an interactive 10-click SAM-Med3D edges it on the larger TotalSegmentator set — and it trails nnU-Net ResEnc L.
Reproduction boundaryPretraining uses 229,619 public CT series and industrial hardware: 24 NVIDIA B200 GPUs across three DGX B200 systems. The paper does not state wall-clock training time.

1. The core problem: where can a 3D transformer afford attention?

Self-supervised learning means learning from the image itself, without human labels. Vision-language alignment means matching images to text descriptions, usually by pulling paired image-text embeddings together. Both ideas work well in 2D vision, but CT changes the cost model and the data model at the same time.

The cost model changes because CT is volumetric. A 2D image patch grid becomes a 3D patch grid. If the patch count becomes large, global self-attention becomes the bottleneck because every token attends to every other token. The paper's root question is therefore not "should we use transformers for CT?" It is sharper: which attention should stay high resolution, and which attention should be compressed?

The data model changes because CT scans have anisotropic geometry and weak clinical text. Anisotropic means that voxel spacing differs by axis, often with thicker slices than in-plane pixels. A model that treats all three axes as the same physical scale can learn the wrong geometry. Clinical reports add another mismatch. A report can mention many findings, omit small findings, and use different wording for the same condition. That makes the usual single-positive, many-negative CLIP setup fragile.

Q: Why not just use smaller patches, local windows, or a CNN-like decoder and call it done?

Guide: separate detail from context. CT tasks need small lesions and organ boundaries, but they also need the full scan context that says where a window sits in the body.

A: A purely local model can keep detail but loses whole-scan context. A purely global transformer keeps context but becomes too expensive at CT resolution. SPECTRE keeps both by assigning them to different stages. The local stage spends tokens inside windows. The global stage receives one compact descriptor per window and rebuilds scan context over that short sequence.

Takeaway: The architecture is a budget allocation. Spend quadratic attention where the token count is fixed and local; compress before the scan-level attention would explode.

2. What the authors actually claim

The stated contributions have three parts. First, SPECTRE introduces a geometry-aware 3D transformer design for CT, using anisotropic 3D patches and 3D rotary positional encoding. Rotary positional encoding, or RoPE, injects position by rotating query and key vectors, so position is represented by a continuous function rather than a learned lookup table.

Second, the paper proposes a two-level attention architecture. The local transformer processes dense 3D windows. The global transformer processes one descriptor per window. This is the mechanism that makes large-scale volumetric transformer pretraining tractable.

Third, the paper uses a two-stage pretraining pipeline. Stage 1 learns visual geometry with DINOv3-style self-distillation, iBOT masked patch distillation, and KoLeo embedding spreading. Stage 2 aligns scan embeddings with radiology reports through SigLIP, using Qwen3-0.6B text embeddings with LoRA, a low-rank adapter method that tunes a small set of added weights instead of the full language model.

The larger inference is about open medical foundation models. The model is trained only on public CT datasets, and the authors release the model, code, and recipes. That matters because many medical foundation models depend on private hospital-scale data. The paper argues that careful scale, architecture, and objective design can close much of that gap with public data, although the corpus is still heavily chest-skewed.

3. Data: 229,619 public CT series, with a chest-heavy distribution

The pretraining corpus contains eight public CT sources after dataset-specific exclusions. All eight are used for self-supervised learning. Three sources with radiology reports or diagnosis codes are used for vision-language alignment. The scale is large for public CT, but the anatomy distribution is not balanced: NLST alone contributes 132,985 chest series.

For self-supervised learning, scans are reoriented to RAS coordinates, clipped to \([-1000,+1000]\) Hounsfield Units, normalized, resampled to \(0.5 \times 0.5 \times 1.0\) mm, and center-cropped to at most \(512 \times 512 \times 384\) voxels. Training samples a \(256 \times 256 \times 128\) crop. Four random crops from the same scan are treated as separate batch elements to improve I/O efficiency.

For vision-language alignment, scans with text are resampled to \(0.75 \times 0.75 \times 1.5\) mm. Reports are split into Findings and Impressions when those sections exist. Each section is rewritten by Gemini 2 Flash into two additional paraphrases, guided by radiologist-curated examples. The prompt asks for clear, concise rephrasing without changing medical facts. During training, one version is sampled. Structured diagnosis codes are not rewritten; they are replaced by WHO 2025 short descriptions and appended after the rewritten report.

Q: Is report rewriting just cleanup, or does it change the learning problem?

A: It changes the invariance the text encoder sees. A report finding can be stated several ways while pointing to the same CT scan. By training on paraphrases, the model learns that wording variation should not move the text embedding far away. Keeping diagnosis-code descriptions outside the rewrite step preserves billing and epidemiological facts that a language model might otherwise distort.

Takeaway: The text pipeline attacks clinical noise from the data side, while SigLIP attacks it from the loss side.

Q: Why is the architecture asymmetric, with 24 local layers but only 4 global layers?

A: The expensive detail lives inside windows, where the token count is controlled. That lets the model spend depth on local anatomy. The global transformer has a different job: connect window summaries. Its sequence length is only the number of windows plus one scan \(cls\) token, so a shallow global transformer can restore scan-level context without reprocessing every patch.

Takeaway: SPECTRE does not make all attention cheap. It makes the expensive attention local, then makes global attention short.

5. Method: the equations behind the two-level transformer

5.1 Anisotropic 3D tokenization

Start with a CT volume \(X \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times D}\). The first stage partitions the volume into non-overlapping 3D patches and projects each flattened patch into a token.

$$T^{(0)}=\big[x_1,\ldots,x_N\big]^{\top},\qquad x_i=E\!\left(P_i(X)\right)\in\mathbb{R}^{d}.$$
Eq. 1: \(P_i(X)\) is the \(i\)-th 3D patch, \(E(\cdot)\) is the linear projection, and \(d=1080\).

The patch size is \(16 \times 16 \times 8\) voxels. The depth is half the in-plane patch size because CT slice spacing is typically about twice the in-plane spacing. This makes the patch cover a more balanced physical extent across axes.

$$\frac{16 \times 16 \times 8}{1080}=\frac{2048}{1080}\approx 1.8962.$$
Compression note: one 2048-voxel patch becomes a 1080-dimensional token, so the tokenizer is conservative rather than aggressively lossy.

For a typical \(128 \times 128 \times 64\) crop, this patching yields 512 tokens. The paper uses that number as a useful anchor: it matches the token count of a \(256 \times 256\) 2D image with \(16 \times 16\) patches.

5.2 Local attention and window descriptors

The local transformer applies standard multi-head self-attention inside one window. If \(T_w \in \mathbb{R}^{(1+m)\times d}\) is the token matrix for window \(w\), including its \(cls\) token, attention is ordinary transformer attention.

$$\mathrm{Attn}(T_w)=\mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V,\qquad Q=T_wW_Q,\quad K=T_wW_K,\quad V=T_wW_V.$$
Eqs. 2-3: local window attention. The window token count \(m\) is fixed, so the scan cost is \(G\) copies of a fixed local cost.
$$\mathrm{Cost}_{\mathrm{global}}=G\cdot O(m^2d).$$
Eq. 4: the paper's per-layer scan cost for fixed window size. The symbol name is copied from the paper; the important scaling is linear in window count \(G\) when window token count \(m\) is fixed.

After the local transformer, each window is compressed into a single descriptor. The model keeps the final \(cls\) token \(c_w\), averages the patch tokens, and concatenates those two summaries.

$$\bar{t}_w=\frac{1}{m-1}\sum_{i=2}^{m}T^{(\ell)}_{w,i},\qquad u_w=\big[c_w\ \|\ \bar{t}_w\big]\in\mathbb{R}^{2d},\quad w=1,\ldots,G.$$
Eqs. 5-6: \(c_w\) is the learned window summary; \(\bar{t}_w\) is the mean of local patch tokens; \(u_w\) is the descriptor passed upward.

This is the key compression step. The global transformer does not see every patch. It sees \(G\) window descriptors, projected back to dimension \(d\), plus a scan-level \(cls\) token.

$$U=\big[u_1,\ldots,u_G\big]^{\top}\in\mathbb{R}^{G\times 2d},\qquad Z=\begin{bmatrix}c_g\\ \tilde{U}\end{bmatrix}\in\mathbb{R}^{(G+1)\times d},\qquad T^{(g)}=\mathrm{ViT}_g(Z).$$
Eqs. 7-9: the global transformer attends over the short scan-level sequence.

Why the descriptor is the load-bearing approximation

The approximation assumes that a window can be summarized by two signals: a learned window summary and the average of its patch tokens. If that summary keeps the anatomical content that matters for downstream tasks, global attention can operate on \(G\) descriptors instead of all patch tokens. If a task needs exact small-structure boundaries across windows, this compression can become the bottleneck.

5.3 3D RoPE and scale jitter

SPECTRE uses 3D rotary positional encoding in both vision transformers. RoPE rotates query and key channels according to continuous coordinates. The head dimension must be divisible by 6 because each of the three axes receives sine and cosine slots.

$$d_k\equiv 0\pmod{6},\qquad L=d_k/6.$$
Eq. 10: \(L\) is the number of frequency slots per axis.

The paper also applies RoPE-box jittering. Normalized coordinates \(r_i\in[-1,1]^3\) are rescaled by \(s\sim U(0.5,2.0)\), giving \(\tilde{r}_i\). With shared frequency periods \(p\in\mathbb{R}^{L}\), each axis gets an angle.

$$\theta_i^{(a)}=2\pi\,\tilde{r}_i^{(a)}/p,\qquad a\in\{h,w,d\},\qquad \cos_i=\cos(\Theta_i),\quad \sin_i=\sin(\Theta_i).$$
Eqs. 11-12: \(\Theta_i\) concatenates the per-axis angle vectors.
$$Q'=R(Q;\cos_i,\sin_i),\qquad K'=R(K;\cos_i,\sin_i).$$
Eq. 13: RoPE rotates query and key heads before attention.

There are two reasons this matters for CT. The positional rule is continuous, so it is less tied to one training grid than a learned absolute position table. The random scale factor trains the model not to rely on one exact coordinate scale, which is useful when voxel spacing and field of view vary across scanners.

5.4 Stage 1: DINO, iBOT, and KoLeo

Stage 1 trains the local transformer with a student-teacher setup adapted from DINOv3. The teacher is an exponential moving average of the student. The model sees two global views and eight local views per CT volume. The optimization combines DINO, iBOT, and KoLeo with weights \(1:1:0.1\). The paper deliberately omits DINOv3's Gram loss, saying it is more relevant when optimizing dense features in billion-parameter models than in the narrower CT domain.

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{DINO}}=-\frac{1}{D}\sum_{g\in\mathcal{G}}\sum_{\substack{v\in\mathcal{G}\cup\mathcal{H}\\ v\neq g}}\sum_{k=1}^{K}q_t^{(k)}(g)\log p_s^{(k)}(v).$$
Eq. 15: \(\mathcal{G}\) is the set of global crops, \(\mathcal{H}\) is the set of local crops, \(D=|\mathcal{G}|(|\mathcal{G}|+|\mathcal{H}|-1)\), and \(K\) is the number of DINO prototypes.
$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{iBOT}}=-\frac{1}{|M|}\sum_{m\in M}\sum_{c=1}^{C}q_t^{(c)}(m)\log p_s^{(c)}(m).$$
Eq. 16: \(M\) is the set of masked patch positions, \(C\) is the token-wise head vocabulary size, and SPECTRE uses \(K=C=65{,}536\) prototypes/classes.
$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KoLeo}}=-\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\log\!\left(\lVert z_i-z_i^{\mathrm{NN}}\rVert_2+\varepsilon\right).$$
Eq. 17: \(z_i\) is an L2-normalized embedding, \(z_i^{\mathrm{NN}}\) is its nearest neighbor in the batch excluding itself, and \(\varepsilon\) is a small stabilizer.

The higher iBOT mask range is not arbitrary. In 3D, each token has more spatial neighbors than in 2D, so masked prediction can become easier. The paper raises the mask range from the DINOv3/iBOT default \(U(0.1,0.5)\) to \(U(0.2,0.7)\) to keep the task informative.

5.5 Stage 2: SigLIP report alignment

Stage 2 aligns full scans to text. Each preprocessed scan is partitioned into \(G=36\) windows of \(128 \times 128 \times 64\) voxels. The image branch produces a \(d=1080\) feature. The text branch uses Qwen3-0.6B Embedding with LoRA adapters of rank \(r=16\) and \(\alpha=64\). Image and text are projected to a shared 512-dimensional space and L2-normalized.

$$\mathrm{sim}(v,t)=\frac{\langle v,t\rangle}{\tau}.$$
Eq. 18: scaled cosine similarity between normalized image and text embeddings.
$$\mathcal{L}_{v\to t}=-\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left[\log\sigma\!\left(\mathrm{sim}(\tilde{v}_i,\tilde{t}_i)\right)+\frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{j\neq i}\log\!\left(1-\sigma\!\left(\mathrm{sim}(\tilde{v}_i,\tilde{t}_j)\right)\right)\right].$$
Eq. 19: image-to-text SigLIP loss, using sigmoid binary terms instead of a softmax over the batch.
$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{SigLIP}}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\mathcal{L}_{v\to t}+\mathcal{L}_{t\to v}\right).$$
Eq. 20: the total loss averages image-to-text and text-to-image directions.

How to read Eqs. 18-20

\(v_i\) and \(t_i\) are the normalized image and text embeddings for the paired scan-report sample, \(\tau\) is the temperature, and \(\sigma\) is the sigmoid. The positive term pulls \(v_i\) toward \(t_i\). The negative sum pushes \(v_i\) away from other text embeddings \(t_j\), but without forcing all candidates into one softmax competition.

Q: Why does the paper prefer SigLIP over CLIP-style softmax contrastive learning?

A: Clinical reports break two assumptions that softmax contrastive learning likes. First, a batch negative can share clinical meaning with the positive because patients often have overlapping findings. A softmax loss treats that shared-meaning sample as a competitor and pushes it down. SigLIP scores each pair with a binary sigmoid term, so the penalty is less globally coupled. Second, 3D scans limit batch size. The VLA batch size is 144, and softmax contrastive learning usually benefits from very large negative sets. Sigmoid losses are less sensitive to small batches, and the paper further shuffles text embeddings across devices to increase negatives without excessive communication.

Takeaway: SigLIP is not a cosmetic loss swap. It relaxes the single-positive and large-batch assumptions that are weak in report-paired 3D CT.

7. Experiments: what transfers, and where?

7.1 Frozen biomarker classification

The biomarker study follows the TumorImagingBench protocol. For each foundation model, the encoder is frozen, embeddings are extracted, and a k-nearest neighbor classifier is trained without finetuning. This isolates representation quality from task-specific optimization.

The six tasks include malignancy prediction on LUNA16 and DLCS, plus two-year survival prediction on NSCLC-Radiomics, NSCLC-Radiogenomics, C4KC-KiTS, and Colorectal-Liver-Metastases. The paper reports that SPECTRE is best on four of the six benchmarks. It also notes that NSCLC-Radiogenomics and Colorectal-Liver-Metastases have large confidence intervals and generally low scores, so those tasks should not be overread as clean wins or losses.

7.2 Semantic segmentation with SEoMT

For dense prediction, the authors adapt Encoder-only Mask Transformer to 3D and call it SEoMT. A fixed set of learnable query tokens, one per semantic class, is appended to the encoder token sequence. The model predicts masks on a \(H/4 \times W/4 \times D/4\) grid and upsamples with trilinear interpolation. This keeps the test focused on encoder quality rather than decoder engineering.

Q: Merlin beats SPECTRE hard on Findings. Does that contradict the paper's alignment claim?

A: No, but it narrows the claim. Merlin was trained on this abdominal-CT distribution (about 15,000 scans) while SPECTRE sees it zero-shot, so Merlin's edge on Findings reflects in-domain training, not a fairer comparison. SPECTRE's stronger evidence is that it beats Merlin on Impressions Recall@1 and improves further when the full report is used. Impressions are less structured and more interpretive than Findings, so this is the part of the table that supports the paper's claim about robustness to report structure.

Takeaway: The retrieval result is not "SPECTRE beats Merlin everywhere." It is "SPECTRE transfers well zero-shot and is especially strong when report sections are less rigid or combined."

Q: What is the easiest way to oversell this paper?

A: Say that SPECTRE is state of the art for all CT tasks. The paper does not show that. The stronger and more accurate claim is that SPECTRE learns a transferable 3D CT representation from public data, performs strongly without finetuning on biomarker tasks, transfers competitively to segmentation with a light encoder-centric head, and establishes useful scan-report alignment.

Takeaway: The model is best understood as an open, scalable CT representation backbone, not as a specialized winner for every downstream task.

10. Closing: the useful mental model

In one sentence: SPECTRE makes a full-volume CT transformer practical by doing expensive attention inside local windows, compressing each window to a descriptor, and doing global attention only over those descriptors.

Three takeaways:
  1. The central architectural move is not "use a transformer." It is deciding which level gets dense tokens and which level gets compressed descriptors.
  2. The training recipe is matched to CT reality: anisotropic patches and 3D RoPE address geometry; DINO/iBOT/KoLeo learn visual structure; SigLIP handles noisy many-condition reports better than a softmax batch competition.
  3. The empirical story is strongest when it is phrased narrowly: broad frozen transfer and strong retrieval, competitive encoder-only segmentation, and clear limits on small lesions, chest bias, and compute.
