X-PCR: can an ophthalmic MLLM finish the whole diagnostic chain?

Paper2Html2026-06-25CVPR 2026 digest entry | Medical AI | Shenzhen University / University of NottinghamSource: arXiv:2604.20350v1 PDF
Medical AIOphthalmology BenchmarkProgressive Clinical ReasoningCross-modal AlignmentUncertainty CalibrationMathJax checked

The short version. X-PCR, short for Cross-Modality Progressive Clinical Reasoning, asks a hard question about multi-modal large language models (MLLMs): can they reason through an ophthalmic diagnosis as a chain, or do they only answer isolated visual question answering (VQA) items? The benchmark builds a six-stage workflow - image quality assessment (IQA), anatomical localization (AL), lesion characterization (LC), disease diagnosis (DD), severity grading (SG), and clinical decision-making (CD) - where later stages depend on validated earlier outputs. It also adds cross-modal reasoning over six ophthalmic modalities: External Photography (EP), Color Fundus Photography (CFP), Fluorescein Angiography (FFA), Indocyanine Green Angiography (ICGA), Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT), and RetCam. The headline dataset has 26,415 expert-verified images and 177,868 VQA pairs from 51 public datasets, plus 58 hospital-collected multi-modal cases for cross-modal testing. The key metric is Chain Completion Rate (CCR): the share of staged cases where all six stages are correct at once. This is where the best model collapses. GPT-5 answers 76.24% of individual stages correctly, but completes only 24.47% of full chains — while specialists complete 62.48%. The per-stage skill is real, and GPT-5 still leads every model there; it just does not survive the linked workflow.

One-line contributionA benchmark that turns ophthalmic diagnosis from independent VQA questions into a dependent six-stage clinical reasoning chain plus explicit cross-modal evidence integration.
Key mechanismThe next question is conditioned on the previous verified answer. That design lets the benchmark measure vertical coherence, error propagation, and self-consistency rather than only per-question correctness.
Core evidenceAmong model rows, GPT-5 has the highest average stage-wise accuracy, 76.24%, but its CCR is 24.47%; no evaluated model reaches attending physicians on overall accuracy, 79.91%.
Reproduction costX-PCR is an evaluation benchmark, not a training recipe. The paper discloses zero-shot deterministic decoding, A100 inference for local models, and API use for hosted models; GPU-hours, wall-clock time, and API costs are not stated in the paper.

1. Motivation: what does isolated VQA miss?

An ordinary medical VQA benchmark can ask whether a fundus image shows a lesion, whether an OCT image contains fluid, or which disease label fits an image. Those questions are useful, but they are usually scored independently. Clinical diagnosis is not independent in that way. A clinician first decides whether the image is usable, then locates anatomy, then describes lesions in that anatomy, then diagnoses, grades severity, and chooses management.

The dependency matters because an answer can be locally correct but globally incoherent. A model might identify diabetic macular edema in one prompt and then choose a management plan that does not follow from the same evidence. Averaging isolated questions hides that break. X-PCR tries to expose it by making each downstream stage inherit the earlier stage's validated output.

Q: Why is the chain stricter than six separate accuracies?

Guide: separate accuracies ask whether each skill exists somewhere. A chain asks whether the same case can move through all skills without contradiction.

A: CCR only counts a case when IQA, AL, LC, DD, SG, and CD are all correct. One wrong or inconsistent stage breaks the chain. That is why a model can have respectable average accuracy and still have a low CCR.

Takeaway: X-PCR is not mainly a larger eye-image quiz; it is a test of whether the outputs stay clinically connected.

2. Contributions: what the paper actually adds

The paper states four contributions. First, it introduces X-PCR as an ophthalmology benchmark with 26,415 expert-verified images, 177,868 VQA pairs, and 52 diseases. Second, it formalizes diagnosis as a six-stage reasoning chain with explicit dependencies. Third, it integrates six ophthalmic imaging modalities with ground-truth cross-modal correspondences. Fourth, it evaluates 21 leading MLLMs and compares them with residents, attendings, and specialists.

The important narrowing is that X-PCR's novelty is not raw scale. GEMeX is larger in VQA count, and OmniMedVQA covers more modalities. X-PCR is different because Table 1 combines four axes: progressive clinical reasoning (PCR), multimodal integrated diagnosis (MID), uncertainty awareness (UA), and expertise-graded evaluation (EGE).

3. Data: where the benchmark comes from

X-PCR has two data sources with different jobs. The public part supplies scale: 51 public ophthalmic datasets, 26,415 images, 177,868 VQA pairs, 52 diseases, and eight disease categories: diabetic retinopathy, glaucoma, cataract, age-related macular degeneration, hypertensive retinopathy, pathological myopia, retinal vein occlusion, and rare conditions. The hospital part supplies cross-modal realism: 58 prospectively collected multi-modal cases with clinical history, synchronized imaging, expert cross-modal correspondences, differential diagnoses, and treatment recommendations.

The public data curation keeps diagnostic quality separate from image quality. Images with original labels and acceptable quality become VQA items. Low-resolution or severely artifacted images are reserved as adversarial samples without quality labels, so the IQA stage can test whether a model knows when an image should not be used for diagnosis.

VQA generation is semi-automated, but not fully automatic. For each stage, ophthalmologists design 2 to 10 multiple-choice templates. For stages 1 to 5, GPT-5 generates VQA items from dataset labels and lesion annotations, and Gemini-2.5-Pro audits consistency and accuracy. Stage 6, clinical decision-making, is manually authored by ophthalmologists because it requires fine-grained management reasoning. A second ophthalmologist independently reviews 20% of VQA items, items below inter-rater agreement threshold kappa less than 0.8 go to adjudication, and persistently disputed items are excluded.

Q: Does GPT-5 generating many questions make GPT-5's evaluation unfairly easy?

A: It is a real caveat. The paper reduces the risk through Gemini-2.5-Pro auditing, ophthalmologist difficulty labels, 20% second-review sampling, and adjudication. But GPT-5 is both a generation tool and one evaluated model, so a model-family bias cannot be ruled out from the PDF alone.

Takeaway: The data construction is clinically checked, but a third-party question-generation ablation would make the benchmark cleaner.

4. Pipeline: two reasoning axes

X-PCR evaluates two axes. The vertical axis is the six-stage chain, where a case must move from image quality to management. The horizontal axis is cross-modal reasoning, where the model must connect evidence across eye-imaging modalities.

Six-stage chain, reconstructed from paper Section 3.1. Each stage answers a different clinical question, and the dependency column explains where error can propagate.
StageWhat the model must doDependency
IQA - Image Quality AssessmentDetect focus, illumination, artifact, and other quality problems; decide whether the image supports confident diagnosis or should be reacquired.Gate for the full pipeline. Non-diagnostic images should not feed confident downstream reasoning.
AL - Anatomical LocalizationIdentify optic disc, macula, vascular arcades, and peripheral retina.Conditioned on IQA. The anatomical map sets where later lesions can be described.
LC - Lesion CharacterizationDescribe lesion morphology and distribution with clinical terms such as flame-shaped hemorrhages or cotton-wool spots.Conditioned on AL. Lesions must be spatially anchored to the localized anatomy.
DD - Disease DiagnosisSynthesize lesion evidence into a ranked differential diagnosis with justification.Conditioned on LC. Incomplete or contradictory lesion evidence broadens or weakens diagnosis.
SG - Severity GradingApply disease-specific grading rubrics, such as the International Clinical Diabetic Retinopathy scale.Conditioned on DD. If the diagnosis changes, the grading rubric changes too.
CD - Clinical Decision-MakingTranslate diagnosis and severity into management: treatment, referral urgency, and follow-up.Conditioned on DD and SG, with uncertainty pushing toward confirmatory testing or conservative action.

The cross-modal axis has three task families. Correspondence identification links semantically equivalent findings across paired modalities, such as CFP macular elevation and OCT serous retinal detachment. Diagnostic integration combines aligned evidence from multiple modalities to infer the most likely diagnosis. Modality selection asks which next imaging modality is most informative when uncertainty remains, such as using ICGA to distinguish central serous chorioretinopathy from Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease.

Q: Why are vertical and horizontal reasoning tested together?

A: Ophthalmic diagnosis needs both. A model can fail vertically by contradicting its own earlier finding. It can fail horizontally by treating additional modalities as noise instead of evidence. X-PCR makes both failures visible.

Takeaway: The benchmark is strongest when read as a shape test: can the model preserve a diagnostic story across steps and modalities?

5. Method: what exactly is scored?

X-PCR reports six evaluation views. Stage-Wise Accuracy (SWA) is per-stage correctness. Chain Completion Rate (CCR) is end-to-end correctness across all six stages. Expertise-Stratified Accuracy (ESA) reports accuracy by resident, attending, and specialist difficulty tiers. Uncertainty-Aware Score (UAS) rewards calibrated confidence and penalizes confident mistakes. Expected Calibration Error (ECE) measures bin-wise mismatch between confidence and empirical accuracy. Modality Contribution Score (MCS) measures how much a modality helps in multi-modal cases.

$$\mathrm{CCR}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\mathbf{1}\big[\mathrm{IQA}_i \land \mathrm{AL}_i \land \mathrm{LC}_i \land \mathrm{DD}_i \land \mathrm{SG}_i \land \mathrm{CD}_i\big]$$
Eq. 1: writing the paper's CCR definition explicitly - a chain counts only when all six stages are correct.

How to read Eq. 1

The symbol \(\land\) means "and." CCR is therefore stricter than an average. A case with five correct stages and one wrong stage contributes zero to CCR, because the clinical chain did not finish.

UAS adds a clinical reliability layer. Each model response includes a self-reported confidence normalized to the interval \([0,1]\). The paper groups responses into four categories: Correct Confident (CC), Correct Uncertain (CU), Incorrect Uncertain (IU), and Incorrect Confident (IC). This is more clinically meaningful than accuracy alone because an uncertain wrong answer can trigger review, while a confident wrong answer can mislead management.

ECE is the calibration companion to UAS. The paper applies confidence calibration on the validation set through temperature scaling and isotonic regression, freezes those parameters for testing, and evaluates calibration with 10-bin ECE. The paper does not print the UAS base-score constants, so this page does not invent them.

Q: Why not rank models only by ECE?

A: ECE measures whether confidence matches accuracy, but it does not by itself encode clinical harm. UAS separates the four cases that matter clinically. Correct and confident is good. Correct and uncertain is less useful. Incorrect and uncertain is safer than incorrect and confident.

Takeaway: UAS asks whether the model should be trusted on a case; ECE asks whether its confidence scale is calibrated.

6. Evaluation setup: no training, controlled inference

X-PCR is not a model-training paper. The evaluated systems are prompted zero-shot with deterministic decoding: temperature 0.0, top-p 1.0, and max new tokens 256, raised to 16,384 for cross-modal tasks. Hosted commercial models are called through APIs under those settings. Local models use fp16 inference on NVIDIA A100 GPUs, with single-GPU inference for models less than or equal to 13B parameters and multi-GPU inference for models at least 30B parameters.

The model set has 21 MLLMs: 6 commercial, 10 open-source, and 5 medical-specialized models. Commercial systems include GPT-5, GPT-5 nano, Claude-Haiku-4.5, GLM-4.5v, Gemini-2.5-Flash, and Gemini-2.5-Pro. Open-source systems include Qwen2.5-VL, Qwen3-VL, InternVL, LLaVA, LLaMA3-LLaVA-Next, and ShareGPT4V variants. Medical-specialized systems include MedGemma-27B-IT, Lingshu-7B, LLaVA-Med-7B, and HuatuoGPT-Vision variants.

The paper does not state GPU-hours, wall-clock time, API cost, or exact hosted-model version pinning beyond the cited model references. That matters for reproduction because commercial model behavior can drift with service updates.

Read Table 2 in three passes. First, GPT-5 is the strongest model by SWA average at 76.24%, but it does not beat the attending baseline at 79.91% or the specialist baseline at 82.85%. Second, the chain view is much harsher than the average: GPT-5 reaches 24.47% CCR, Qwen2.5-VL-72B reaches 13.77%, and many models sit below 1%. Third, the difficulty tiers matter: GPT-5 is at 80.64% on resident-level questions but 62.92% on specialist-level questions, below attendings at 68.27% and specialists at 83.63% on the same tier.

The table does not support a blanket "accuracy always decreases stage by stage" claim for every row. Some rows rise at intermediate stages, such as Qwen3-VL-8B moving from AL 64.67 to LC 75.80. The honest pattern is broader: the later clinical stages, especially SG and CD, are much harder, and CCR shows that local correctness rarely survives the whole chain.

8. Ablation logic: is the chain measuring dependence or just difficulty?

X-PCR is a benchmark paper, so its ablation evidence is mostly in the metric design and in the modality removal table. The first possible objection is that later stages are simply harder. That is partly true; CD and SG are clinically harder than IQA. But difficulty alone does not explain why average accuracy and CCR diverge so sharply.

Q: How can we tell dependence apart from ordinary task difficulty?

Guide: difficulty lowers per-stage accuracy. Dependence creates a stricter event: all stages must be right on the same case.

A: Table 2 shows both effects. Per-stage scores tell us later tasks are hard. CCR tells us the whole chain rarely survives. If the benchmark only measured difficulty, SWA would be enough. The extra collapse in CCR is the signal that stage errors and inconsistencies are accumulating across a linked workflow.

Takeaway: SWA measures local skill; CCR measures whether local skill remains coherent across the case.

The second ablation view is MCS in Table 4. If a model truly integrates modalities, removing CFP, OCT, FFA, ICGA, or EP should usually hurt when that modality is relevant. The printed table is noisier: some removals help, some hurt, and the signs vary by model and group. That does not prove a specific modality is useless. It does show that current MLLMs do not use additional modalities in a stable, clinician-like way on the 58 hospital cases.

Q: What is the most likely overstatement?

A: "MLLMs cannot diagnose eye disease." The paper supports a narrower claim: under X-PCR's dependent six-stage and cross-modal tests, current MLLMs do not match attending-level performance and rarely complete the full reasoning chain.

Takeaway: The benchmark is a strong diagnostic reasoning stress test, not a final clinical verdict on every ophthalmic MLLM use case.

10. Summary: the useful mental model

In one sentence: X-PCR turns ophthalmic VQA into a linked clinical workflow, and that change makes the gap between single-step competence and end-to-end reasoning visible.

Three takeaways

  1. The key metric is CCR. It counts the whole case, so it exposes broken chains that average stage accuracy can hide.
  2. The cross-modal result is not "more images always help." On 58 hospital cases, adding modalities often lowers accuracy or produces unstable MCS signs, which points to weak evidence fusion.
  3. The human comparison matters. GPT-5 leads model rows in average accuracy, but no model reaches attendings on SWA average, and specialists remain far ahead on CCR.

A good reading path through the paper is: read Section 3.1 for the six-stage chain, then Table 2 for the CCR collapse, then Table 4 for the cross-modal fusion failure. Those three pieces are the core argument.
